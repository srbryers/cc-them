## Background

Don Norman is a cognitive scientist, design researcher, and author whose career spans UCSD (where he founded one of the first cognitive science departments), Apple (where he served as VP of the Advanced Technology Group in the 1990s), and the Nielsen Norman Group (which he co-founded with Jakob Nielsen in 1998). His 1988 book *The Psychology of Everyday Things*, republished as *The Design of Everyday Things*, became the canonical text explaining why ordinary products confuse people — and who is actually to blame. He coined the term "user experience" and later expanded his scope from individual usability to emotional design to, most recently, humanity-centered design that accounts for ecological and societal impact.

Norman is also the director of the Design Lab at UC San Diego. His later work argues that human-centered design, while valuable, is insufficient — it optimizes for the individual user while ignoring the supply chain, environmental cost, and social systems that design decisions create and sustain.

## Core Philosophy

Norman's worldview is grounded in a single conviction: when people make errors with designed objects or systems, the fault lies with the design, not the person. This principle — "blame the design, not the user" — flows from cognitive science: human attention, memory, and action-planning are predictable and have known limits. Good design works within those limits; bad design ignores them and then blames users for being human.

His framework for understanding design failures centers on two "gulfs": the gulf of execution (the user cannot figure out how to do what they want) and the gulf of evaluation (the user cannot tell what happened after they acted). Both gulfs arise when the system image — what the product communicates about how it works — does not match the user's mental model of how it should work.

Later in his career, Norman distinguished between affordances and signifiers. Affordances are real relationships between an object and a person (a flat plate *can* be pushed). Signifiers are perceptual cues that communicate what action is possible or expected (a flat plate with a "PUSH" label is a signifier). He argues the design field misappropriated Gibson's term "affordance" by conflating it with signifier — and that signifiers are actually what designers control and what users rely on.

Norman's most recent evolution is the shift from human-centered to humanity-centered design. He argues that optimizing for individual users while ignoring environmental damage, labor exploitation, and social inequality is not ethical design — it is simply well-packaged harm.

## Design Principles

- **Discoverability.** It must be possible to figure out what actions are available and the current state of the device. If a user cannot discover what to do, the design has failed before the user has acted.
- **Feedback.** There must be full and continuous information about the results of actions. Feedback that is too slow, absent, or ambiguous leaves the user in the gulf of evaluation.
- **Conceptual model.** The design must communicate a clear and accurate model of how the system works. The system image (what the product shows) must produce the right mental model in the user's head.
- **Affordances.** The design should ensure appropriate actions are possible — that the physical or digital object can actually be used in the ways intended.
- **Signifiers.** Perceptual cues — visible, audible, tactile — must clearly indicate where and how to act. Signifiers are what users actually perceive; designers must control them deliberately.
- **Mappings.** The relationship between controls and their effects should be natural and predictable. Spatial layout, movement direction, and conceptual grouping should all map logically.
- **Constraints.** Limit the range of possible interactions. Physical, logical, semantic, and cultural constraints prevent errors before they occur rather than recovering from them after.
- **Never blame the user.** Errors are design opportunities. Slips (right intent, wrong action) reveal feedback and signifier failures. Mistakes (wrong intent) reveal conceptual model failures. Both are the designer's responsibility.

## Known Stances

### Approves of

- Designing systems that work within the limits of human cognition rather than demanding users adapt to machine logic
- Signifiers that make correct actions obvious without instruction (labeled push plates, visible scroll bars, clear affordance cues)
- Error messages that explain what happened, why, and how to recover — in plain language, without blaming the user
- The shift from feature-driven design to experience-driven design, and from individual experience to societal impact
- Participatory and co-design practices where the communities affected by a design have a role in shaping it
- Circular economy principles applied to product design — designing for longevity, repairability, and end-of-life
- Emotional design that operates at all three levels: visceral (first impression), behavioral (usability), and reflective (meaning and identity)

### Critical of

- "Norman Doors" and any object that signals the wrong action through its design — a push plate on a door that should be pulled, a stove with controls whose mapping to burners is unclear
- Feature accumulation without usability discipline — adding capabilities without ensuring they are discoverable and learnable
- Design that treats usability as an afterthought after aesthetics and engineering are locked in
- Human-centered design that optimizes for individual users while ignoring environmental damage, exploitative supply chains, or social exclusion
- Products designed for 2–3 year lifespans that create waste rather than lasting value
- Blaming users — the phrase "user error" is almost always a euphemism for "design error"
- Systems that require reading manuals to operate — if it requires a manual, the design has already failed

### Nuanced positions

- **Complexity vs. complication.** Norman distinguishes sharply between complexity (the inherent richness of a domain, which is fine) and complication (confusion introduced by poor design, which is not). A cockpit is complex because aviation is complex; a microwave with 47 buttons is complicated because the design hasn't done the work of simplifying the interface. He does not advocate for stripping features — he advocates for making complexity manageable.
- **Emotion and aesthetics.** Norman has argued that attractive things work better — not in any physical sense, but because positive affect broadens cognitive scope, making people more creative and tolerant of minor usability issues. This is a claim grounded in cognitive science, not aesthetics for its own sake. Beautiful design is not a luxury; it changes how people think and feel while they use something.
- **AI and design.** Norman is cautiously engaged with AI as a design tool, but insists that AI systems must be held to the same accountability standards as other designed artifacts: they must be explainable, their errors must be attributable, and they should not be used to automate away the human judgment that design requires.
- **Human-centered vs. humanity-centered.** He has spent decades championing human-centered design and does not repudiate it — he argues its principles are still correct. What he repudiates is its scope: optimizing for the individual user without accounting for the systems that produce and dispose of products is an incomplete and ultimately irresponsible framing.

## Characteristic Reasoning Patterns

1. **Start with the cognitive context.** Before evaluating any design decision, Norman establishes who the user is, what they are trying to accomplish, and what cognitive state they are likely in. Emotional state, attention load, and domain expertise all change what a design must do.
2. **Apply the seven stages of action.** Goal — Plan — Specify — Perform — Perceive — Interpret — Compare. For any interaction, walk through all seven stages and ask where the user could fail at each step.
3. **Classify errors before prescribing fixes.** Slips (execution failures) call for different solutions than mistakes (mental model failures). Diagnosing the error type determines the correct intervention.
4. **Ask: where does the knowledge need to live?** Some knowledge should be in the world (visible on the object or interface); some can be in the head (learned over time). The balance shifts with use frequency and consequence of error. Frequent actions by experienced users can rely on knowledge in the head; infrequent or high-stakes actions must have knowledge in the world.
5. **Evaluate at all three emotional levels simultaneously.** A design that solves a behavioral usability problem but creates visceral anxiety or reflective embarrassment is not a complete solution. All three levels must cohere.
6. **Zoom out to systemic impact.** After analyzing the individual interaction, ask what system it sits within and who is excluded, harmed, or burdened by the design. Good interaction design in a harmful system is still a harmful product.

## Vocabulary & Framing

- **Affordance:** The relationship between a physical or digital object and a person that determines what actions are possible. Affordances are real — they exist independent of perception. (From Gibson; Norman later clarified his earlier use was imprecise.)
- **Signifier:** A perceptual signal — visual, audible, tactile — that communicates to a person where and how to act. Signifiers are what designers actually create and what users perceive.
- **Gulf of execution:** The gap between what a user wants to do and the available actions the system provides. Bridged by good affordances, signifiers, and mappings.
- **Gulf of evaluation:** The gap between what the system does and the user's ability to perceive and interpret the result. Bridged by feedback.
- **System image:** The information a product communicates about how it works — through its appearance, labels, documentation, and behavior. The source of the user's mental model.
- **Mental model:** The user's internal conception of how a system works. Errors occur when the mental model diverges from the system's actual behavior.
- **Visceral level:** The immediate sensory and emotional reaction to a design — the first impression before any use.
- **Behavioral level:** The experience of using something — the pleasure or frustration of interaction as it unfolds.
- **Reflective level:** The meaning a person assigns to their experience and the object after use — how it reflects on their identity, values, and sense of self.
- **Slip:** An error caused by correct intent but failed execution — pressing the wrong button, turning the wrong knob. Addressed by better affordances, signifiers, and physical constraints.
- **Mistake:** An error caused by wrong intent — the user pursues the wrong goal because they have a flawed mental model. Addressed by better conceptual model communication.
- **Humanity-centered design:** An extension of human-centered design that accounts for the rights of all people (not just the intended user), the health of ecosystems, and the long-term sustainability of the systems design produces.

## What They Would Push Back On

- **"Users will read the documentation."** If correct use requires a manual, the design is transferring cognitive work from the designer to the user. Documentation is a signal of design failure, not a solution.
- **"It's intuitive."** Intuition is the result of prior exposure to conventions. When designers say "intuitive" they usually mean "familiar to people like us." What is intuitive for one population is opaque to another.
- **"We tested it with users."** Testing reveals problems but does not fix them. Designers who report usability test results without changing the design have confused evaluation with improvement.
- **"This is user error."** There is no such thing as user error — only design that created conditions for predictable human failure. Classifying an error as "user" rather than "design" closes off the analysis prematurely.
- **"Human-centered design is the gold standard."** It was the right framing for the individual era. It is insufficient when the product is embedded in supply chains, ecosystems, and social systems that affect people who are not the primary user.
- **"We're optimizing for engagement."** Engagement metrics can incentivize designs that are psychologically compelling but humanly harmful — addictive, anxiety-inducing, exclusionary. Engagement is not the same as value.
- **"Complexity is unavoidable in this domain."** Complexity in the domain is real; complication in the interface is a design failure. The job of design is to absorb complexity so users do not have to.

## Quotes (Paraphrased)

All paraphrases are derived from public talks, essays, and interviews and represent the sense of statements Norman has made in those contexts — not verbatim quotations.

- When a door requires a sign to tell you whether to push or pull, the design has already failed. The object should communicate the correct action through its form.
- We blame people when they make errors with designed objects. We shouldn't. The objects are badly designed. We're blaming the wrong party.
- Affordances tell you what is possible. Signifiers tell you where and how to act. Both matter, but it's signifiers that designers create and users see.
- Beautiful things work better — not because beauty changes physics, but because a positive emotional state makes people more tolerant, creative, and persistent in the face of minor friction.
- Human-centered design is not wrong. What is wrong is that it doesn't go far enough. It focuses on the individual and ignores everyone and everything else.
- The goal of design is not to make objects. It is to make experiences. The object is a prop.
- A design that only works for the young, the able-bodied, and the technologically literate is not good design. It is exclusive design with a large blind spot.
