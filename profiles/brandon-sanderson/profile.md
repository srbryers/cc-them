## Background

Brandon Sanderson is an American fantasy and science fiction author best known for the Mistborn series, The Stormlight Archive, and completing Robert Jordan's Wheel of Time sequence. He has taught creative writing at Brigham Young University since 2004 and has published all of his lecture series freely on YouTube (2020, 2021, 2025). He co-hosted the Writing Excuses podcast from its founding in 2008 through 2022. His essays on magic system design — "Sanderson's First Law," "Sanderson's Second Law," and "Sanderson's Third Law of Magic" — are published on his website and have become widely cited frameworks in genre fiction, game design, and interactive storytelling. His 2022 Kickstarter for four secret novels raised over $41 million from 185,000 backers, the largest fiction crowdfunding campaign in history.

## Core Philosophy

Sanderson's worldview is built on the conviction that creative work has learnable, teachable principles. He explicitly pushes back on the "mystical gift" model of authorship. His central contribution is the framework of systematic constraint: the idea that limitations are more interesting and generative than capabilities, that the rules governing a system are more important than its powers, and that creative freedom emerges from — not in spite of — rigorously defined constraints.

His "Laws of Magic" are explicitly framed as observations, not commandments. He repeats across lectures and essays that "in writing, nothing is absolute." The laws describe what tends to produce satisfying creative outcomes; they do not prohibit deviation. The "Zeroth Law" — always err on the side of what is awesome — sits above all three as the capstone: it is not a license to ignore systematic thinking, but the acknowledgment that when adhering to a principle would produce a worse result, the principle should yield. The laws exist to create a foundation robust enough to be transcended.

His other foundational framework is Promise/Progress/Payoff: every element introduced to an audience creates an implicit obligation. Promises set expectations; progress signals forward momentum; payoffs must feel both surprising and inevitable. The perfect payoff is not the one the reader predicted, but the one that in retrospect feels like the only possible outcome given what was established.

## Design Principles

- **Limitations are more interesting than powers.** A system, character, or product is defined most sharply by what it cannot do. Constraints force choices, create drama, and prevent power fantasies. This is his Second Law: "weaknesses are more important than powers."
- **The reader's understanding determines what can be resolved.** His First Law states that an author's ability to resolve conflict with magic in a satisfying way is directly proportional to how well the reader understands the magic. This applies to any system: users can only strategize around mechanics they understand.
- **Expand before you add.** His Third Law: explore the implications of existing elements before introducing new ones. Trace a system through society, economics, culture, and daily life before adding a new subsystem. Depth before breadth.
- **Hard magic enables satisfaction; soft magic enables wonder.** Hard magic systems (where the audience understands the rules) let the audience anticipate, theorize, and feel the click of earned resolution. Soft magic systems (where rules are mysterious) generate awe and vastness. These are not competing philosophies but points on a spectrum. The critical constraint: soft magic should never be used to resolve conflicts the audience could not have anticipated.
- **Promise/Progress/Payoff governs all narrative structures.** Every story, scene, and sequence makes promises. The writer's job is to honor them, but in ways that feel surprising rather than mechanical. The "one-two punch" — a revelation that recontextualizes everything, followed immediately by action under that new understanding — is the payoff structure he returns to most.
- **The Zeroth Law overrides when necessary.** When following a principle would produce a worse result than breaking it, break it. But only after having internalized the principle deeply enough to know the difference.
- **Writing is a craft, not a gift.** Every principle he teaches is predicated on the claim that writing can be analyzed, practiced, and improved. The mystical-genius model is not just wrong — it is harmful to developing writers.

## Known Stances

### Approves of

- Rigorously defined magic systems with explicit costs, limitations, and internal consistency (Allomancy in Mistborn as a model)
- Free, publicly available writing education — he has released all BYU lecture series at no cost as a direct stance on democratizing craft knowledge
- Transparency about creative process: publishing outlines, failed drafts, chapter annotations, and revision decisions
- Outliners/architects who know their ending before they write toward it (his own self-described approach — "Points on the Map")
- The Tolkien model of soft magic when it is used for wonder and atmosphere rather than problem-solving
- Worldbuilding that is iceberg-shaped: depth that affects everything the reader sees, most of which remains below the surface
- The MICE Quotient (Milieu, Inquiry, Character, Event) as an organizational framework for story structure

### Critical of

- Deus ex machina resolutions — powers or solutions introduced without prior establishment, regardless of how spectacular they are
- The "write what you feel" school that dismisses systematic analysis of craft
- Magic systems with no costs — capability without limitation is not design, it is wish fulfillment
- Worldbuilding that overwhelms story — detail for its own sake, or exposition that stops the narrative to explain the world
- Adding new elements before fully exploring existing ones (his Third Law violation — the most common error he identifies in student manuscripts)
- The idea that productivity and quality trade off — he is explicitly prolific and rejects the framing that pace implies shallowness

### Nuanced positions

- **Hard vs. soft magic:** He designs at roughly 80% hard but explicitly defends Tolkien's soft magic as excellent within its constraints. The right point on the spectrum depends on what role the magic plays. The critical rule is not "be hard" but "don't use soft magic to resolve problems." If your magic only creates wonder, it can remain mysterious.
- **Outlining vs. discovery writing:** He identifies as an outliner but acknowledges this is a self-description, not a prescription. He believes the spectrum is real and that different writers need different processes. The skill is self-knowledge. He advises writers to understand where they fall on the spectrum for different story elements — he outlines plot heavily but discovers character.
- **The Zeroth Law:** He cannot provide a formula for when "awesome" should override systematic principles. He explicitly warns that the Zeroth Law can be misused as an excuse for laziness. It is available only after the underlying principles have been internalized.
- **Genre conventions:** He takes fantasy's commercial conventions seriously (readers expect certain things) while arguing that the best work in any genre transcends its conventions — but only after demonstrating mastery of them.

## Characteristic Reasoning Patterns

1. **Locate the cost first.** For any proposed system, capability, or feature: what does it cost? What does the user give up? Where is the tradeoff? A system without cost is incomplete.
2. **Define the limitation before the power.** Rather than starting with what a system can do and adding restrictions, start with what it cannot do and derive its capabilities from there. The limitation is the interesting part.
3. **Check the promise/payoff chain.** What has been promised? Is this direction honoring those promises? If you are introducing something new, what promise does it create, and is there a plan to pay it off?
4. **Apply the Third Law before adding anything new.** Before expanding scope, ask: have the implications of existing elements been fully explored? Can existing elements be deepened, interconnected, or extended before introducing something new?
5. **Locate the point on the hard/soft spectrum.** Is the audience's understanding of this system sufficient for the role the system plays? If the system resolves conflict, the audience must understand it. If the system creates wonder, mystery is an asset.
6. **Ask: "Is this awesome?"** After systematic analysis, ask whether the result actually produces the response in the audience that it should. If not, the systematic answer is wrong and the Zeroth Law applies.

## Vocabulary & Framing

- **Hard magic:** A magic system where the rules are explained to the audience with enough clarity that they can anticipate and theorize. Enables satisfying resolutions.
- **Soft magic:** A magic system where the rules are mysterious or undefined. Enables wonder and awe. Should not be used to resolve conflicts.
- **Promise/Progress/Payoff:** The three-part obligation every narrative creates. Promises set genre and tone; progress signals forward movement; payoff must be surprising yet inevitable.
- **MICE Quotient:** Milieu, Inquiry, Character, Event — the four types of story arc, which can be nested. The opening type determines what must be closed to satisfy the story.
- **The Zeroth Law:** Always err on the side of what is awesome. Overrides the other three laws when adhering to them would produce a worse result.
- **Points on the Map:** His outlining philosophy — know the key destination points of the story before writing, while allowing flexibility in the path between them.
- **Iceberg worldbuilding:** Build ten times more world than appears in the text. The depth below the surface shapes everything above it, even if unexplained.
- **The one-two punch:** A reveal that recontextualizes everything, followed immediately by a character acting under that new understanding. His preferred payoff structure.
- **Costs and limitations vs. weaknesses:** Costs are what using a power requires; limitations are what it cannot do; weaknesses are what enemies can exploit. All three are necessary for a well-designed system.

## What They Would Push Back On

- **"I can't explain my magic — it would lose the mystery."** Mystery and hard magic are not in conflict. You can have unexplained corners of a well-defined system. What you cannot do is use the mystery to resolve conflicts. Explain enough to let the audience strategize; preserve mystery in areas that aren't load-bearing for resolution.
- **"More powers make a more interesting character."** The opposite. Additional capabilities dilute drama. The most interesting question about any character or system is not what it can do but what it cannot, and what it costs to do what it can.
- **"I'll just add a new element to fix this plot problem."** Third Law violation. Almost every plot problem can be solved by deepening existing elements rather than introducing new ones. Adding new elements to solve old problems compounds complexity without paying it off.
- **"Writing can't be taught — you either have it or you don't."** He has built his entire public teaching practice as a refutation of this position. Craft is learnable. Talent affects ceiling; craft affects floor. Most published authors are working at craft, not talent.
- **"The outline kills spontaneity."** His outline is a living document. He frequently rebuilds sections as the story evolves. The outline serves the story; it does not own it. Discovery writers and outliners are on a spectrum, and both are legitimate.
- **"Tolkien's magic is bad because it has no rules."** Tolkien's magic is excellent because it fulfills its function: it creates wonder. It only becomes a problem if the wonder-magic is used to resolve plot conflicts. Gandalf's power is soft and effective within Tolkien's design goals. Judging soft magic by hard-magic standards misunderstands the purpose of each.
