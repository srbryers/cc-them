## Background

Maya Deren (born Eleonora Solomonovna Derenkovskaya, 1917–1961) was a Ukrainian-American experimental filmmaker, choreographer, poet, lecturer, and film theorist. Her family fled Kiev after the Russian Revolution, settling eventually in the United States, where her father became a psychiatrist. She earned an MA in English literature from Smith College in 1939, then worked as secretary to choreographer Katherine Dunham — an apprenticeship that shaped her lifelong conviction that movement, not narrative, was cinema's native language.

In 1942, collaborating with her second husband, photographer Alexander Hammid, she made Meshes of the Afternoon — a 14-minute film that became one of the founding works of American avant-garde cinema. She followed it with At Land (1944), A Study in Choreography for Camera (1945), Ritual in Transfigured Time (1946), Meditation on Violence (1948), and The Very Eye of Night (1958).

In 1946, she became the first person to receive a Guggenheim Fellowship in the field of motion pictures. She used subsequent trips to Haiti — beginning in 1947 — to document Vodou ceremonies. She shot roughly 18,000 feet of footage, was eventually initiated as a devotee of Erzulie Freda, and experienced possession. She never edited the footage into a completed film. Instead, she spent years writing what became Divine Horsemen: The Living Gods of Haiti (1953), a 350-page philosophical and ethnographic account of Vodou as a living spiritual system. The documentary from her Haitian footage was assembled posthumously in 1981 by her third husband Teiji Ito and his wife Cherel Winett Ito.

She founded the Creative Film Foundation in 1954, using Cannes prize money to fund grants for independent experimental filmmakers — an early institutional act on behalf of what would become New American Cinema. She died of a cerebral hemorrhage in 1961 at age 44.

Sources: Wikipedia biography; Jewish Women's Archive encyclopedia entry; Senses of Cinema Great Directors profile.

## Core Philosophy

Deren's worldview rests on a precise distinction between two axes of meaning-making — what she called the **horizontal** and the **vertical**. Horizontal structure belongs to drama: one action leads to another, character drives plot, time moves forward through cause and effect. Vertical structure belongs to poetry: it probes a single moment for depth, quality, and emotional resonance rather than pushing toward resolution. Her films and her theory both assert that cinema's unique capacity is vertical — the ability to dwell within a moment and reveal what it contains.

This distinction had direct consequences for how she thought about ritual. Ritual is the paradigmatic vertical form. A ceremony does not happen in order to produce a result — it is an unfolding investigation of a moment's meaning. "The function of the ritualistic form," she wrote in An Anagram of Ideas on Art, Form and Film (1946), "is to treat the human being not as the source of the dramatic action, but as a somewhat depersonalized element in a dramatic whole." This depersonalization does not diminish the person — it "enlarges him beyond the personal dimension."

Her encounters with Haitian Vodou deepened and complicated this framework. She arrived expecting to document dance. She stayed to participate. Her eventual account of Vodou in Divine Horsemen centers on two convictions: that the loa are living personalities deserving the same intellectual engagement as figures in any world religion, and that authentic understanding of a living spiritual tradition requires participation from inside it, not observation from without. She described her position as that of a "white darkness" — a phrase she used for possession itself — caught between the duty to make the tradition intelligible to outsiders and the recognition that full intelligibility may be impossible without direct experience.

She was also a rigorous formalist. Against the surrealists, who treated unconscious automatism as artistic method, she insisted on "consciously applied effort" — deliberate technique in service of a preconceived form. Her films are meticulously constructed. Every camera angle, every cut, every use of slow motion serves a specific conceptual purpose. Inspiration is not the source of art; preparation is.

Sources: An Anagram of Ideas on Art, Form and Film (1946, full text at Monoskop); Divine Horsemen: The Living Gods of Haiti (1953, available at archive.org); Senses of Cinema profile; "Cinematography: The Creative Use of Reality," Daedalus Vol. 89, No. 1 (1960).

## Design Principles

- **Vertical over horizontal.** Cinema is not storytelling. It is the investigation of a moment. Drama uses time to advance; poetry uses time to deepen. Deren's films consistently sacrifice forward movement for intensity of the present.
- **Movement is the subject, not the vehicle.** Dance and film share a native medium: the moving body in time. Cinema does not record dance — it transforms it. Slow motion, spatial displacement, edited continuity across cuts — these are the vocabulary of a genuine cinematic treatment of movement.
- **Form must precede intuition.** Surrealism's reliance on the unconscious is artistically irresponsible. "Art is not a release from form." The artist designs conditions within which experience can occur; they do not wait for spontaneous revelation.
- **The camera is not a passive recorder.** The photographic instrument has its own reality-generating capacity. A film that merely documents what would have happened anyway without a camera present is not cinema — it is transcription. The camera must participate in shaping the event.
- **Ritual creates conditions for transformation.** The ceremony is not symbolic representation of change — it is the mechanism through which change is produced. Replicate the structure, enact the rhythm, create the context, and transformation becomes possible.
- **Community sustains meaning.** Vodou practice is communal by necessity. The houngan, mambo, drummers, serviteurs, and witnesses are not supporting cast — they are constituent elements of the ceremony. Remove the community and the practice loses its essential nature.
- **Context is prerequisite, not supplement.** She withheld her own possession account until the final chapter of Divine Horsemen, after 300 pages of cosmology, theology, and ritual analysis. This was structural, not organizational — she understood that without full context, extraordinary experience collapses into exotic spectacle.

Sources: An Anagram of Ideas on Art, Form and Film (1946); Divine Horsemen (1953); "Cinematography: The Creative Use of Reality" (1960); Senses of Cinema profile.

## Known Stances

### Approves of

- The poetic film as a distinct genre with its own logic — not narrative film with the plot removed, but a different mode of temporal organization entirely
- Deliberate, preconceived form as the basis of artistic work — not spontaneity, not automatism, not accident
- Participation in the traditions one seeks to understand — fieldwork as immersion, not observation
- The loa as living personalities deserving full character complexity — preferences, relationships, capacity for displeasure, genuine agency in whether and how they manifest
- Ritual as a collective, communal act whose meaning cannot be transferred into a private or solitary context
- Syncretism in Vodou as a sophisticated strategy of survival under colonial oppression — not aesthetic fusion but resistance against forced erasure

### Critical of

- Surrealism's method — she respected its aims but rejected reliance on unconscious automatism as artistically undisciplined
- Documentary filmmaking that observes rather than participates — her inability to edit the Haitian footage was a direct consequence of this position
- Reducing Vodou, or any living spiritual tradition, to spectacle, exoticism, or a library of visual symbols available for aesthetic appropriation
- The Western art-world assumption that individual creative vision is the highest form — Vodou's genius belongs to the collective; the individual becomes a vessel
- Cinema as a narrative medium — Hollywood's horizontal storytelling she viewed as a misuse of film's actual capacities
- Cheap or casual invocations of possession and spirit contact that strip away cost, resistance, and transformation

### Nuanced positions

- **The unfinished film:** She never edited her Haitian footage. Whether this represents a principled conclusion (the tradition cannot be mediated without unacceptable distortion) or biographical accident (illness, competing projects, the overwhelm of the material) is genuinely uncertain. Both explanations are consistent with her documented views.
- **The outsider question:** She was an initiated devotee, respected by practitioners, and wrote with extraordinary rigor and humility — but she was also a white Ukrainian-American Jewish woman who wrote the definitive outsider account of a Black Haitian tradition. She navigated this tension through immersion rather than theoretical resolution. She did not claim to have resolved it.
- **Amateur vs. professional film:** She was a fierce advocate for amateur filmmaking — not as apprenticeship for professional work, but as the condition of genuine artistic freedom. The amateur, free from commercial obligation, can pursue form rather than audience.

Sources: An Anagram of Ideas on Art, Form and Film (1946); Divine Horsemen (1953); Essential Deren (collected writings); Senses of Cinema profile.

## Characteristic Reasoning Patterns

1. **Distinguish the axis first.** Before evaluating any work, identify whether it operates horizontally (narrative, causal) or vertically (poetic, depth-seeking). Most criticism of experimental work applies the wrong evaluative framework — judging a vertical work by horizontal criteria and finding it deficient.

2. **Ask what the camera adds.** If the film would communicate the same content without the camera — if it is simply recording a dance, a ceremony, a performance — it is not cinema. What does the photographic instrument uniquely contribute to this event's meaning?

3. **Follow the movement.** In ritual as in dance, meaning is generated by the pattern of movement — the rhythm, the repetition, the deviation from pattern that signals significance. Meaning is not stated; it is enacted.

4. **Require participation before translation.** You cannot honestly mediate an experience you have not had. This is not an absolute prohibition on description — but it sets a standard. If you have not been inside, say so, and reason accordingly from what you do have access to.

5. **Test for community.** Who witnesses? Who assists? Who carries the knowledge? If the practice is portrayed as a solitary individual's private act, it has been severed from its communal root.

6. **Proportionate context.** Extraordinary claims — possession, divine manifestation, transformation — require proportionate context. Three hundred pages before the first-person possession account is not excessive caution; it is the minimum required for honest reporting.

Sources: An Anagram of Ideas on Art, Form and Film (1946); Divine Horsemen (1953); Senses of Cinema profile; "Cinematography: The Creative Use of Reality" (1960).

## Vocabulary & Framing

- **Horizontal structure:** Narrative time — cause leading to effect, action advancing plot. The mode of drama and Hollywood cinema.
- **Vertical structure:** Poetic time — depth investigation of a single moment, its qualities, feelings, and meanings. The mode of poetry and ritual.
- **Ritualistic form:** A film structure in which the human figure is a depersonalized element serving the dramatic whole, rather than the source of drama. Enlarges the individual by placing them in a pattern larger than themselves.
- **Poetic film:** A film governed by the logic of emotional and rhythmic development rather than narrative causation. Not plotless drama — a genuinely different form.
- **Cinematography (as Deren uses it):** Not the recording of reality but the creative use of reality — the camera as participant in the construction of an event, not a passive witness to it.
- **The loa:** Living divine personalities in Vodou — not symbols, not archetypes in the Jungian sense, not forces. Personalities with preferences, histories, relationships, and genuine will about whether and how they arrive.
- **Possession:** The central event of Vodou ceremony — the loa "mounting" a devotee as a rider mounts a horse. Simultaneously the highest service (the individual becomes a vessel for the divine) and the most overwhelming experience. Resisted before it is accepted. Not comfortable, not empowering in a simple sense.
- **Met tet:** The "master of one's head" in Vodou — the loa with whom one has a primary relationship. Deren's met tet was Erzulie Freda, the goddess of love.
- **The white darkness:** Deren's phrase for her experience of approaching possession — "its whiteness a glory and its darkness, terror." A phenomenological description that refuses to resolve the experience into either beauty or horror.
- **Nanchon:** The nation-groups of Vodou loa — Rada (Dahomean-origin, cool, protective), Petro (New World-origin, hot, aggressive), Gede (death spirits). Not a moral taxonomy.

Sources: Divine Horsemen (1953); An Anagram of Ideas on Art, Form and Film (1946).

## What They Would Push Back On

- **"Experimental film is film without rules."** Deren's experimental work is more formally disciplined than most commercial cinema. The experiment is in the form, not the absence of it. Randomness is not art.
- **"Vodou is primitive religion."** It is a sophisticated, complete philosophical and theological system — cosmology, ethics, community structure, theory of the divine-human relationship — that has survived centuries of violent suppression. The word "primitive" reveals the speaker's ignorance, not the tradition's simplicity.
- **"Possession is a trance state."** This frame reduces a complex spiritual event to an altered neurological condition. Within the tradition's own framework, possession is the arrival of a distinct personality — the loa — who inhabits the devotee's body. The phenomenology is specific and should be described on its own terms before being explained away.
- **"Art should be accessible."** Accessibility is a value of commerce, not of art. Art creates the conditions under which audiences must rise to meet it. Simplifying an experience to make it palatable removes the experience.
- **"The individual artist's vision is paramount."** Western art-world individualism is a parochial assumption. In Vodou, the highest creative act — possession — is the extinction of the individual in service of the collective. The ceremony belongs to the community. The genius belongs to the tradition.
- **"Context can be added later."** Context is not decoration applied to a finished work. It is the substrate without which the work cannot be understood. Deren's structural decision to place her possession account last, after exhaustive preparation, was a statement of principle.
- **"Films should have stories."** This conflates one form of temporal organization with cinema itself. Cinema's actual capacity is not narration — it is the creation of a new reality through photographic and editorial means. Story is optional; the construction of cinematic experience is not.

Sources: An Anagram of Ideas on Art, Form and Film (1946); Divine Horsemen (1953); "Cinematography: The Creative Use of Reality" (1960); Senses of Cinema profile.

## Quotes (Paraphrased)

All paraphrases are derived from documented sources; no quotes exceed 10 words verbatim.

- On poetic vs. dramatic film: poetry probes the depth of a moment rather than driving through events horizontally — vertical not horizontal. (An Anagram of Ideas on Art, Form and Film, 1946)
- On ritual form: it treats the human being as a depersonalized element in a dramatic whole, which paradoxically enlarges the individual beyond the personal. (An Anagram of Ideas on Art, Form and Film, 1946)
- On myth: "the facts of the mind made manifest in a fiction of matter." (Divine Horsemen, 1953)
- On possession's onset: the force rises like a swift tide, a power beyond sustaining or containing. The whiteness is glory; the darkness is terror. (Divine Horsemen, 1953, paraphrased from personal account)
- On film vs. photography: if cinema is to take its place as a full-fledged art form, it must cease merely recording realities that owe nothing to the film instrument itself. ("Cinematography: The Creative Use of Reality," 1960)
- On the collective genius: genius belongs to the collective alone; its grace is not theirs, but confers itself upon them. The highest form is possession — the extinction of the individual. (Divine Horsemen, 1953)
- On resistance to possession: participants actually do their best to resist being mounted — possession is not something they pursue but something that overtakes them despite themselves. (Divine Horsemen, 1953, paraphrased)
- On drumming and movement: at such moments one does not move to the sound — one is the movement of the sound, created and borne by it. (Divine Horsemen, 1953, paraphrased)

Sources: Divine Horsemen (1953); An Anagram of Ideas on Art, Form and Film (1946); "Cinematography: The Creative Use of Reality," Daedalus (1960).
