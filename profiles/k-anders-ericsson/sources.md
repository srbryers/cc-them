# Sources: K. Anders Ericsson

All sources are freely available public recordings, transcripts, articles, or open-access papers. No paywalled sources are listed.

## Books

- [Peak: Secrets from the New Science of Expertise — Wikipedia summary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peak:_Secrets_from_the_New_Science_of_Expertise) — Overview of the 2016 book co-authored with Robert Pool. The definitive public synthesis of Ericsson's 30-year research program on deliberate practice, the three-tier practice taxonomy, and mental representations.

## Research Papers (Open Access)

- [Deliberate Practice and Proposed Limits on the Effects of Practice on the Acquisition of Expert Performance: Why the Original Definition Matters — Frontiers in Psychology, 2019](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2019.02396/full) — Ericsson & Harwell's final peer-reviewed defense of deliberate practice's original definition against looser interpretations. Clarifies naive vs. purposeful vs. deliberate practice taxonomy and responds to critics who conflated the terms.

- [Deliberate Practice and Acquisition of Expert Performance: A General Overview — Academic Emergency Medicine, 2008](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1553-2712.2008.00227.x) — Ericsson's accessible overview of deliberate practice applied to medical training. Covers mental representations, feedback requirements, and the coach's diagnostic function.

- [The Role of Deliberate Practice in the Acquisition of Expert Performance — APA PsycNet record (Psychological Review, 1993)](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1993-40718-001) — The foundational 1993 paper with Krampe and Tesch-Römer. Introduced the deliberate practice construct, the violin study, and the finding that accumulated deliberate practice — not innate talent — distinguished expert from sub-expert performers.

## Articles & Essays

- [The Making of an Expert — Harvard Business Review, July 2007](https://hbr.org/2007/07/the-making-of-an-expert) — Co-authored with Neil Charness and Paul Feltovich. The most widely read accessible statement of Ericsson's framework. Covers deliberate practice, the role of coaches, mental representations, and the limits of experience alone as a predictor of expertise.

- [K. Anders Ericsson (1947–2020) — Florida State University Department of Psychology memorial](https://psychology.fsu.edu/article/k-anders-ericsson-1947-2020-worlds-foremost-expert-expert-performance) — FSU's official memorial page. Summarizes Ericsson's career, research contributions, and the scope of his influence on expertise research across medicine, music, sports, and chess.

- [K. Anders Ericsson — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K._Anders_Ericsson) — Comprehensive biography covering his academic career, major publications, the deliberate practice framework, and documented critiques of his positions including the talent debate and the 10,000-hour misrepresentation.

## Interviews & Podcasts

- [Achieving Peak Performance: A Conversation with Anders Ericsson — Behavioral Scientist](https://behavioralscientist.org/achieving-peak-performance-a-conversation-with-anders-ericsson/) — Substantive interview covering the science behind Peak, the distinction between naive/purposeful/deliberate practice, what coaches actually do, and why self-directed practice is limited. One of the best publicly available long-form treatments of his framework.

- [Beyond 10,000 Hours of Practice: What Experts Do Differently — Knowledge at Wharton Podcast](https://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/podcast/knowledge-at-wharton-podcast/anders-ericsson-book-interview-peak-secrets-from/) — Ericsson explains what the 10,000-hour rule gets wrong, what his violin study actually showed, and what the type of practice — not the quantity — determines about expert development.

- [Anders Ericsson: Dismantling the 10,000 Hour Rule — Good Life Project Podcast](https://www.goodlifeproject.com/podcast/anders-ericsson/) — Ericsson walks through his critique of Gladwell's misreading and explains the core principles of his framework in accessible terms. Covers the difference between deliberate and purposeful practice and the role of mental representations.

- [Dr. Anders Ericsson: Secrets from the New Science of Expertise — Jordan Harbinger Show](https://www.jordanharbinger.com/dr-anders-ericsson-secrets-from-the-new-science-of-expertise/) — Wide-ranging interview on skill acquisition, innate talent vs. deliberate practice, feedback design, and how to apply the framework outside domains with established training traditions.

- [Dr. Anders Ericsson: Science of Expertise — The Art of Charm, Episode 513](https://theartofcharm.com/podcast-episodes/dr-anders-ericsson-science-expertise-episode-513/) — Interview focused on how deliberate practice applies to social and interpersonal skill development — directly relevant to the agent's application domain. Covers coach-guided vs. self-directed practice, motivation through challenge, and why some experts plateau or regress.

- [Anders Ericsson: The Science of Expertise — Finding Mastery Podcast](https://findingmastery.com/podcasts/anders-ericsson/) — Interview with sports psychologist Michael Gervais. Covers the psychological architecture of deliberate practice, mental representations in high-performance contexts, and the role of the coach in elite athletic development.

## Obituaries & Retrospectives

- [Remembering the "Father of the 10,000-Hours Rule" — David Epstein](https://davidepstein.com/father-of-the-10000-hours-rule-passes-away/) — Retrospective by the author of Range, who was a notable intellectual interlocutor of Ericsson. Covers the genuine tensions in Ericsson's framework, his position on the talent debate, and his scientific legacy.
