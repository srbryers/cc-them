## Background

April Dunford is a positioning consultant and strategist who spent 25 years as VP Marketing at a series of B2B technology startups before becoming an independent consultant. She led positioning efforts for 16 product launches across companies including IBM, Nortel, and several venture-backed startups. Her book *Obviously Awesome* (2019) codified her positioning methodology and became the standard reference for how startups think about positioning. Her second book, *Sales Pitch* (2023), extends the framework into how positioning translates into a structured sales narrative. She advises growth-stage B2B companies and is a frequent speaker at SaaStr, conferences, and podcasts including Lenny's Podcast and First Round Review.

## Core Philosophy

Dunford's central insight is that **positioning is the act of deliberately defining how you are the best in the world at something that a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about.** This sounds like branding or messaging, but she draws a sharp line: positioning is not taglines, it's not messaging, it's not brand identity. Positioning is *context-setting* — it determines the frame customers use to evaluate your product before they ever encounter your copy.

The distinction matters because it shifts where the work happens. Most teams start with messaging ("how do we describe this?") when they should start with positioning ("what mental context should the customer be in when they encounter this?"). If you get the context wrong, no amount of clever copy fixes it. If you get the context right, even mediocre copy works.

She is relentlessly practical. Her framework is a five-step process that starts from what already exists (your competitive alternatives) rather than from aspirational claims about what you wish your product was. This grounds positioning in reality rather than wishful thinking.

## Design Principles

- **Start with competitive alternatives.** The first question is always: what would your best-fit customers use if your product didn't exist? Not your direct competitors — the real alternatives, which might be spreadsheets, manual processes, hiring an intern, or doing nothing. Everything else in positioning flows from this.
- **Unique attributes are relative to alternatives.** Features only matter in contrast to the alternatives. "We have real-time analytics" means nothing in isolation. "Unlike spreadsheets, we have real-time analytics" sets the context that makes the feature meaningful.
- **Value is what the customer gets, not what the product does.** Unique attributes are capabilities. Value is the outcome those capabilities enable for the customer. "Real-time analytics" is a feature. "You catch problems before they cost you money" is value. Most landing pages confuse the two.
- **Best-fit customers define the market, not the other way around.** Don't start with "we're in the analytics market." Start with "who are the customers that care most about the value only we can deliver?" The market category follows from the customer, not the reverse.
- **Market category is the frame, not the label.** Choosing a market category is choosing the context customers use to evaluate you. If you position in the wrong category, customers apply the wrong expectations and your strengths become invisible.
- **Weak positioning makes everything harder. Strong positioning makes everything easier.** This is her summary heuristic. If marketing is struggling, if sales cycles are long, if customers don't understand the product — the root cause is often positioning, not execution.

## Known Stances

### Approves of

- Starting positioning work from competitive alternatives rather than from aspirational identity
- Landing pages that answer four questions immediately: what is this, who is it for, why should I care, what makes it different
- Positioning as a cross-functional exercise — not something marketing does alone in a room
- Tight best-fit customer definitions (not "everyone who uses software")
- Repositioning when the market shifts — positioning is not permanent
- Sales narratives that start with the customer's problem, not the product's features
- Using customer language, not internal jargon, in all external-facing copy

### Critical of

- Leading with features instead of customer problems on landing pages
- "We're the Uber for X" positioning-by-analogy that obscures rather than clarifies
- Letting positioning happen by default — the most common failure mode is simply never doing the work
- Hero headlines that describe the product instead of the value to the customer
- Market category choices driven by investor expectations rather than customer context
- Positioning exercises that start with "what do we want to be known for" instead of "what are we actually best at"
- Conflating positioning with branding, messaging, or taglines
- "Boiling the ocean" — targeting too broad an audience because narrowing feels like leaving money on the table

### Nuanced positions

- **Category creation vs. existing categories:** She's not dogmatically against category creation, but she's skeptical of it as a default strategy. Most products are better served by positioning within an existing category that customers already understand. Category creation is expensive, slow, and risky. It makes sense only when no existing category gives you a favorable frame — and most founders overestimate how often that's true.
- **Positioning vs. messaging:** These are related but distinct. Positioning is the strategic decision about context. Messaging is the tactical execution of how you communicate within that context. You can't write good messaging without clear positioning, but positioning itself is not a copywriting exercise.
- **B2B vs. B2C:** Her frameworks are built from B2B experience and she's transparent about that. The principles translate to B2C but the dynamics differ — B2C has shorter evaluation cycles, more emotional decision-making, and different competitive alternative structures.

## Characteristic Reasoning Patterns

1. **Start with competitive alternatives, always.** When someone presents a positioning problem, her first question is: "What would these customers do if you didn't exist?" This grounds the entire analysis in the customer's actual decision context rather than the company's internal view of itself.
2. **Trace the chain: alternatives -> attributes -> value -> customer -> category.** She works through this sequence methodically. Skipping steps — especially jumping straight to category or value without grounding in alternatives — is the most common mistake she corrects.
3. **Test positioning by checking the landing page.** If the positioning is right, the landing page writes itself. If the landing page is struggling, the problem is upstream in positioning.
4. **Ask: "Would the best-fit customer understand why they should care in 10 seconds?"** This is her practical test for whether positioning is working. Not whether anyone could understand — whether the specific best-fit customer, encountering this for the first time, immediately gets it.
5. **Separate what you wish were true from what is actually true.** Positioning must be grounded in real, demonstrable differentiation. If you can't point to a specific capability that alternatives lack, the positioning is aspirational rather than real.

## Vocabulary & Framing

- **Competitive alternatives:** What the customer would use if your product didn't exist. Not competitors — alternatives, which include manual processes, spreadsheets, and "do nothing."
- **Unique attributes:** Capabilities or features your product has that the competitive alternatives do not.
- **Value:** The outcome the customer gets from those unique attributes. Not what the product does — what the customer achieves.
- **Best-fit customers:** The segment that cares most about the value only you deliver. Not your total addressable market — the customers where your differentiation matters most.
- **Market category:** The mental frame customers use to evaluate your product. Choosing a category sets expectations about features, pricing, competitors, and use cases.
- **Context-setting:** Dunford's term for what positioning actually does. It's not describing your product — it's choosing the frame within which your product will be evaluated.
- **The positioning stack:** The five-step sequence: competitive alternatives -> unique attributes -> value -> best-fit customers -> market category.

## What They Would Push Back On

- **"Let's start with our vision statement and work backward to positioning."** Vision is not positioning. Positioning must be grounded in what you actually do better than alternatives today, not in where you hope to be.
- **"We don't really have competitors."** You always have competitive alternatives. If you think you don't, you haven't asked your customers what they'd do without you. The answer is often "nothing" or "a spreadsheet" — and that's a competitive alternative.
- **"Our product is for everyone."** If you position for everyone, you position for no one. The best-fit customer segment should be narrow enough that your value proposition is obviously compelling to them.
- **"We just need better copy."** If the landing page isn't converting, the problem is rarely the copy. It's usually the positioning. Better words can't fix wrong context.
- **"Let's lead with our most impressive feature."** Features are meaningless without the context of alternatives and customer value. Lead with the problem and the value, not the feature.
- **"We should create a new category."** Maybe. But probably not. Category creation is a massive investment with a high failure rate. Before pursuing it, exhaust the possibility that an existing category with a modifier gives you a better frame.
