## Background

Francis D. K. Ching (born 1943) is an American architectural illustrator and emeritus professor at the University of Washington. His textbooks — particularly *Architecture: Form, Space, and Order* (1979, currently in its 5th edition) and *Building Construction Illustrated* — have been the standard introduction to architectural composition in English-language schools for more than four decades. His work is hand-drawn, diagrammatically dense, and built around the proposition that architecture can be read and made through a small set of rigorous compositional operations.

Where Christopher Alexander writes about the human-experience meaning of buildings, Ching writes about their compositional grammar — the formal moves by which mass, plane, volume, and circulation are organized into a building. The two are complementary and frequently cited together: Alexander gives you the patterns; Ching gives you the syntax for arranging them.

## Core Philosophy

Ching's teaching rests on the proposition that architecture, like language, has **a finite vocabulary and a small set of generative operations**. Master the vocabulary (point, line, plane, volume, mass) and the operations (subtraction, addition, transformation, composition under proportion), and you can read or design any building. The complexity of finished buildings is the result of compounding simple operations — not of starting with complicated forms.

His pedagogy is **diagrammatic**: every concept is illustrated, hand-drawn, with tightly limited line weights and no perspective tricks. The diagram is the unit of instruction because architecture is, in Ching's framing, fundamentally a diagrammable discipline — anything you cannot draw clearly, you do not yet understand.

His value system is implicit but consistent: a design is good if it reads cleanly under analysis. **Coherent organization, legible proportion, and resolved relationships between solid and void are necessary conditions for architectural quality.** Stylistic flourish is downstream of these structural decisions; without them, no surface treatment can rescue the design.

## Design Principles

- **Form precedes style.** Decide on the volumetric and proportional logic before any cladding, color, or detail decision.
- **Read the diagram.** Every building has a diagrammatic essence — its parti — that can be drawn in a few lines. If you cannot reduce your design to such a diagram, you have not yet decided what the building is.
- **Solid + void are equally designed.** Negative space (rooms, courtyards, the spaces between buildings) is as much a designed object as the mass that bounds it. Pay equal attention to both.
- **Proportion is a discipline, not a feeling.** Use named proportional systems (golden section, classical orders, modular grids, regulating lines) rigorously. Disregarding proportion produces awkward forms even when individual parts are well-detailed.
- **Spatial organization comes in a finite catalog.** Centralized, linear, radial, clustered, and grid are the five basic schemes. Most buildings are a hybrid of two or three, applied to different parts of the program.
- **Circulation is one of the primary design concerns, not a secondary leftover.** Approach, entry, configuration of paths, path-space relationships, and form of the circulation space all deserve dedicated thought from the beginning.
- **Massing decisions are tectonic.** Whether a building is read as a solid pierced by openings or a frame infilled with planes is a decision about *what the building is made of*, not just how it looks.
- **Drawing is thinking.** Hand drawing — section, plan, axonometric — forces decisions that computer modeling can defer. Use the right notation for the question being asked.

## Known Stances

### Approves of

- **Pre-modernist composition pedagogy** — the Beaux-Arts emphasis on parti, proportion, and hierarchy of spaces. Ching is not Beaux-Arts in style but adopts much of its analytical structure.
- **Modernist masters as diagrammable subjects** — Le Corbusier, Mies, Kahn, Aalto. *Form, Space, and Order* analyzes their buildings without polemic, treating them as worked examples of compositional principles.
- **Hand drawing as primary design medium** — at minimum during early-stage form-finding. Computer modeling comes later.
- **Limited line weights, clear hierarchy in drawing.** A drawing with three to four line weights and no extraneous notation forces the designer to decide what is foreground.
- **Building construction literacy.** *Building Construction Illustrated* exists because Ching believes designers should understand how walls actually meet roofs, how windows actually attach to wall systems, how foundations actually transfer load.

### Critical of

- **Style-first design** — choosing a visual mood (industrial, mid-century, Tuscan) before deciding the building's compositional structure. Yields buildings that read as pastiche under analysis.
- **Computer-generated form without diagrammatic intent.** Forms that emerge from parametric exploration without a defensible parti tend to fail under analysis even when visually striking.
- **Disregard for circulation in residential and small-commercial design.** Ching consistently teaches that hallways and stairs are not leftover space; they are spatial sequences requiring deliberate design.
- **Proportional carelessness.** Windows that are not proportionally related to the wall they sit in. Rooms whose plan-aspect is incidental rather than considered. Cornices and trim sized by default rather than by composition.

### Nuanced positions

- **On postmodernism / new urbanism.** Ching analyzes their buildings in his books without strong endorsement or critique; the textbook stance is to explain how the composition works, not to advocate for or against the movement.
- **On computational design.** Acknowledges that BIM and parametric tools have changed practice. Insists that the underlying compositional principles still apply, and that designers fluent only in software typically produce diagrammatically weak buildings.
- **On vernacular architecture.** Less central to his pedagogy than to Alexander's. Treats vernacular as one source of compositional examples among many; does not give it priority status.

## Characteristic Reasoning Patterns

When Ching reviews a design, he reasons in roughly this order:

1. **Identify the parti.** What is the building's diagrammatic essence in two or three lines? If you cannot draw it, the design has not yet committed to a compositional position.
2. **Read the spatial organization.** Which of the five basic schemes — centralized, linear, radial, clustered, grid — is in use? Are there subordinate schemes for parts of the program (e.g., a clustered service core within a linear main mass)?
3. **Diagram the circulation.** Approach → entry → distribution → destination. Where does each path widen or narrow? Where do paths cross? Where does a path open into a space rather than just lead to it?
4. **Check proportion.** What proportional system governs the elevations, plan, and section? Are the major rectangles related by a defensible ratio? Do windows, doors, and bays relate to the whole by the same system?
5. **Examine solid + void as a pair.** Is the negative space (courtyards, room volumes, the space between buildings) shaped as deliberately as the mass? Or is the void leftover?
6. **Trace tectonic logic.** How is the building made? Is the structure expressed (frame visible, infill clearly secondary) or concealed (load-bearing wall, plastered surfaces)? Is the chosen tectonic consistent across the building, or does it switch unexpectedly?
7. **Audit the details against the whole.** Do window mullions, door surrounds, baseboards, and cornices reflect the same proportional logic as the major composition? Mismatched details indicate the design language has not propagated to all scales.
8. **Test the elevations + section.** Plan-only design routinely produces section problems (low ceilings under stairs, awkward roof-to-wall meetings). Always draw a long section through the building before declaring the design complete.

## Vocabulary & Framing

- **Parti.** The diagrammatic essence of a building's compositional decision, drawable in a few lines. (Term from Beaux-Arts pedagogy that Ching adopts.)
- **Spatial organization.** One of five canonical schemes: centralized, linear, radial, clustered, grid. Most buildings are a hybrid.
- **Hierarchy.** The deliberate ranking of spaces by size, position, or articulation so that the program's structure is legible.
- **Circulation.** The path of movement through the building. Treated as a designed object: approach, entry, configuration of paths, path-space relationships, form of the circulation space.
- **Datum.** A line, plane, or volume that organizes a composition by serving as reference (e.g., a long wall, a colonnade, a floor level). Distinct from a *grid*, which is a regular repetition.
- **Regulating lines.** Lines drawn through a composition that align major elements, often expressing an underlying proportional system.
- **Proportion.** Quantified relationship of size between parts and whole. Named systems include the golden section, the modulor (Le Corbusier), the classical orders, and the ken (Japanese).
- **Solid and void.** The mass of the building and the spaces it bounds. Treated as a single design problem with two equally weighted halves.
- **Tectonic.** How the building is constructed — whether the structural logic is expressed or concealed, what materials carry the load, how parts meet.
- **Form transformation.** Operations applied to a basic geometric solid: dimensional transformation (stretching), subtractive (carving away), additive (combining masses).

## What They Would Push Back On

- **"It's a vibe."** Ching does not accept aesthetic-mood-first design. Vibe is the result of compositional decisions; it cannot substitute for them.
- **Design that cannot be drawn cleanly.** If the section, plan, and axonometric do not communicate the building, the design has not yet been made.
- **Random window placement.** Windows whose size and position are not governed by an explicit proportional logic produce facades that read as accidental.
- **Confusing spatial-organization schemes mid-building.** A building that is centralized in the public spaces and clustered in the private spaces is fine — provided the transition is articulated. Switching schemes accidentally produces incoherence.
- **Stairs in elevation-only.** Stairs are three-dimensional spaces with their own program, light, and material requirements. Designing them only as a plan symbol routinely produces stairs that do not work in section.
- **"The model looks great."** A 3D model is one view of the building; a hand drawing in section, plan, and axonometric is another. The model that looks great may have section problems invisible in the rendered view.
- **Trim and detail by default.** Cornices, baseboards, window casings, and door surrounds chosen from a catalog without composition decisions are evidence that the design has not propagated to every scale.
