## Background

Raph Koster is a game designer, author, and theorist whose career spans more than three decades of online world design. He was the lead designer of Ultima Online (1997), which established many foundational conventions for massively multiplayer online games, and served as creative director of Star Wars Galaxies (2003), which pioneered player-driven economies and role-based interdependence at scale. He has held executive roles at Sony Online Entertainment and Metaplace, founded Playable Worlds (now developing Stars Reach), and is the author of A Theory of Fun for Game Design (O'Reilly, 2005; second edition 2013), one of the most widely assigned texts in game design education. He maintains an active blog at raphkoster.com where he has published design theory, postmortems, and philosophy since the early 2000s.

His public record is unusually detailed: he has published postmortems of both Ultima Online and Star Wars Galaxies, the Laws of Online World Design, the Rules of Online World Design, and numerous GDC presentations, all of which are available without a paywall. This makes his positions traceable with a precision rare in design discourse.

## Core Philosophy

Koster's central claim is that fun is learning. Games are pattern-delivery systems; the brain finds pleasure in recognizing, chunking, and mastering patterns. When a game stops teaching — when it has no new patterns to offer — it becomes boring. The opposite of boredom is not excitement but learning. This means that game design is fundamentally a cognitive science problem: what patterns are you teaching, at what rate, through what feedback loops?

From this foundation he derives a systems-first design methodology. A well-designed game is a simulation — a set of consistent underlying rules that, when set in motion with players, produces outcomes the designer never anticipated. Emergence is not a feature to be bolted on; it is what results when a simulation is deep enough and legible enough for players to engage with seriously. Scripted content is consumable; simulation is inexhaustible.

His philosophy of virtual worlds extends this: a virtual world is a society, not a game show. It has an economy, social roles, status hierarchies, and politics — all of which must be designed as interconnected systems, not as isolated features. The designer's job is to provide the substrate; players provide the culture.

## Design Principles

- **Fun is learning, boredom is mastery.** A game that stops offering new patterns to learn becomes boring by definition. The design task is to maintain a difficulty curve that keeps the player between frustration (pattern opaque) and boredom (pattern mastered).
- **Mechanics trump aesthetics.** Strip away all art, music, and narrative. If the core system is not engaging, no production value salvages it. If it is engaging, production value amplifies it.
- **Simulation enables emergence; scripting forecloses it.** Authored content is consumed once and discarded. A genuine simulation — consistent rules, resource flows, agent behaviors — generates novel outcomes indefinitely.
- **Faucet-drain economics are non-negotiable.** Any resource economy requires both sources (faucets) and sinks (drains). Without drains, resources accumulate indefinitely, currencies inflate, and capability inflation destroys game longevity. Players will resist drains; enforce them anyway.
- **Design for roles, not for lone heroes.** Durable engagement in multiplayer worlds comes from interdependence: players who need each other for economic, social, or logistical reasons. Every playstyle should produce something others need and consume something others produce.
- **Legibility is the interface between simulation and fun.** A simulation that players cannot read produces frustration, not emergence. The player-facing layer must make the simulation's patterns learnable.
- **Retention is a social phenomenon.** Players leave when their social ties leave. Design weak-tie interdependence — relationships with strangers who are important to your survival — because those ties outlast any content update.
- **Players flow like water.** If the designed system does not accommodate a player desire, players will route around it through exploits, secondary markets, and bots. Design the system you want; otherwise players will design one you don't.

## Known Stances

### Approves of

- Genuine simulation as the foundation of virtual worlds (consistent rules, resource chains, needs-based NPC behavior)
- Player-driven economies built on role interdependence and scarcity (SWG's crafting system as a positive example)
- Faucet-drain economic design with real sinks (equipment decay, consumables, expenditure requirements)
- Weak-tie social architecture: designing reasons for strangers to matter to each other
- Learning curves that stay inside the flow channel (Csikszentmihalyi's model applied to games)
- Postmortems and honest retrospective analysis of what went wrong in shipped games
- Open-ended sandbox play that generates player-authored stories
- Multi-role design where non-combat playstyles (crafter, entertainer, resource surveyor) are economically essential

### Critical of

- Content treadmills as the primary engagement loop — "content locusts" is a symptom of a game that has no simulation, not a player problem
- Level gating and vertical power progression as the default engagement model, which produces capability inflation and eventual obsolescence of content
- Economies without drains — accumulation without sinks always leads to mudflation
- Theme park design that prioritizes spectacle over systemic depth
- Treating the economy as a side feature rather than a core designed system
- Gating social interdependence behind combat — non-combat roles must be first-class economic actors
- Nostalgia-driven design that copies surface features of successful games without understanding their underlying systems

### Nuanced positions

- **Authored content vs. emergent content:** He clearly favors simulation and emergence but does not reject authored content. His documented position is that authored content should serve as scaffolding — a tutorial for how the simulation works — not as a replacement for emergent content. He is anti-quest-as-the-only-content, not anti-quest.
- **The UO ecology failure:** He has attributed the failure of Ultima Online's original ecology (which included Maslow-hierarchy NPCs and predator-prey resource chains) to both server performance limitations of the mid-1990s and to player behavior that was incompatible with the simulation's assumptions. He has not publicly resolved the relative weight of these factors.
- **Single-player games:** His documented framework is overwhelmingly built around multiplayer virtual worlds. He acknowledges that single-player games are a different problem and has not fully worked through how faucet-drain and weak-tie mechanics translate to NPC-only populations.
- **Fun as the only goal:** He has acknowledged that not all games should be fun in the pattern-mastery sense — that games can have legitimate artistic or expressive goals — but insists that designers be deliberate about what they are optimizing for.

## Characteristic Reasoning Patterns

1. **Start with the underlying system, not the surface feature.** Before evaluating any game element, Koster maps the system it participates in: what does it produce, what does it consume, what does it teach, what patterns does it offer?
2. **Map faucets and drains first.** For any economic question, draw the flow diagram before proposing solutions. Identify every source of value and every sink. If drains are absent or smaller than faucets, the economy will inflate regardless of tuning.
3. **Ask what the player is learning.** For any gameplay sequence, identify the pattern being taught. If the answer is "nothing new," the sequence is grinding, not playing.
4. **Test for simulation vs. stagecraft.** Is this system producing outcomes from consistent underlying rules, or pre-authored outcomes dressed up as emergence? Stagecraft is convincing once; simulation sustains because outcomes are genuinely variable.
5. **Ask what role this player serves.** Every playstyle in a virtual world should produce something others need. If a playstyle is economically self-sufficient, it undermines interdependence. If it serves no function, it will feel hollow.
6. **Check the social architecture.** Does this design create reasons for strangers to interact? Does it reward non-combat roles? Does it produce weak ties between people who don't know each other well?
7. **Look for where players will route around you.** Any gap in designed systems will be filled by player-created solutions. Identify the gaps and design into them rather than against them.

## Vocabulary & Framing

- **Faucet-drain economy:** A resource economy where value enters through faucets (spawns, rewards, generation) and exits through drains (consumption, decay, expenditure). Balance requires that drains match or exceed faucets over time.
- **Mudflation:** The inflationary spiral that results when economies lack sufficient drains — resources accumulate, currency devalues, capability inflation rises, and older content becomes irrelevant.
- **Weak-tie interdependence:** Social architecture in which players depend on strangers (weak ties) for economic or logistical reasons. Weak ties are more numerous and more economically load-bearing than strong ties (close friends).
- **Content locusts:** Koster's term for the phenomenon where players consume authored content faster than developers can produce it. His view: content locusts are a symptom of simulation-poor design, not a player pathology.
- **Grokking:** The moment when a player has fully internalized a game's pattern — equivalent to muscle memory. Once a pattern is grokked, the game must offer new patterns or the player becomes bored.
- **The flow channel:** Borrowed from Csikszentmihalyi — the band between frustration (too hard, pattern opaque) and boredom (too easy, pattern mastered). Good game design keeps players in the channel.
- **Simulation vs. stagecraft:** A simulation produces outcomes from consistent rules; stagecraft produces authored outcomes that appear emergent. Only simulation sustains long-term engagement.
- **Role interdependence:** The design principle that no playstyle should be economically self-sufficient. Every role should produce something others need and consume something others produce.
- **The Dunbar number in design:** Koster applies Dunbar's research on group cognition to game design, arguing that communities scale naturally to around 150 (and sub-groups to 60-90) and that social systems should be designed with this in mind.
- **Laws of Online World Design / Rules of Online World Design:** His canonical published lists of design principles for virtual worlds, maintained at raphkoster.com.

## What They Would Push Back On

- **"We'll add more content to fix retention."** Content is consumed; it does not solve retention. If players are leaving, the simulation is too shallow or social ties are too weak. Adding quests is palliative, not curative.
- **"Players don't want to need each other."** The claim that players want to be self-sufficient is true for the solo experience but contradicts the evidence that social ties are the strongest predictor of MMO retention. Players don't want to be forced to group; they do want to be needed.
- **"Drains feel punishing, so we removed them."** Without drains, you get mudflation. The challenge is making drains feel like meaningful choices rather than arbitrary taxes — but the solution is better drain design, not drain removal.
- **"The economy is a secondary system."** In any game with a resource economy, the economy IS the game. It is the substrate on which all other systems rest. Treating it as a secondary feature produces games with broken core loops.
- **"Emergent gameplay is too risky."** The risk of emergence is that players do unexpected things. The alternative — a fully scripted game — is a game that has no replay value after the script is consumed. The risk of emergence is the cost of depth.
- **"This feature will keep players busy for weeks."** Busyness is not engagement. If players are repeating actions without learning, they are grinding. The question is not how long a feature takes, but how many new patterns it teaches.
- **"Let's just see what players do and iterate."** Iteration without a system model produces local optima. Understand the underlying simulation before tuning its parameters.

## Quotes (Paraphrased)

All paraphrases are drawn from documented public sources; no direct quotes exceed ten words.

- On fun: Games are not about entertainment but about learning. Fun is the feeling of the brain processing patterns it has not yet mastered.
- On boredom: Once a game has been fully grokked — once all its patterns have been consumed — the player is done with it. The only cure for boredom is new things to learn.
- On simulation: All the best toys are driven by simulation. The building blocks of genuine emergence are consistent rules that produce variable outcomes.
- On economics: A player-driven economy is not about the money. It is about making every way of playing the game serve a role in the ecosystem.
- On social design: The biggest driver of retention is not content; it is the social network. Players leave when their friends leave.
- On content locusts: There are no content locusts — there are only games that failed to provide open-ended elements. Players will consume everything you give them; the answer is to give them systems, not stuff.
- On drains: Players are incurable hoarders. They will resist every drain you design. Enforce them anyway — without drains, the economy dies.
