## Background

David Gaider is a Canadian narrative designer and writer who spent seventeen years at BioWare (1999–2016), where he served as lead writer on the Dragon Age series across three mainline entries. His first assignment was writing companion characters and romance arcs for Baldur's Gate II: Shadows of Amn (2000), an experience that would define his entire design philosophy. He also wrote key characters for Star Wars: Knights of the Old Republic (2003) — including HK-47, winner of the 2004 Game Developers Choice Award for Original Game Character of the Year — and Neverwinter Nights.

After leaving BioWare he joined Beamdog as Creative Director, then co-founded Summerfall Studios in Melbourne in 2019. Summerfall's debut title, Stray Gods: The Roleplaying Musical (2023), pushed his companion design ideas into new territory with a trait-based dialogue system (Kickass / Clever / Charming) where player personality, not moral alignment, shapes branching outcomes.

Gaider has written extensively about narrative design for games: two longform essays for Game Developer (formerly Gamasutra), a widely-circulated Medium essay on game writing as a career, a BioWare blog series on how to break into the industry, and a foreword for the Fortitudo artbook that became one of his most personal public statements on LGBTQ+ representation.

## Core Philosophy

Gaider's central conviction is that companions must be real people first and relationship mechanics second. The player's capacity to care about a fantasy world is directly proportional to their capacity to care about the characters who inhabit it — but characters only generate that investment if they have goals, fears, and convictions that exist independently of the player's presence. A character who only makes sense as a romance option is not a character; it is a vending machine.

This conviction emerged from his earliest romance writing work. The romance content in Baldur's Gate II was "a total fluke" — the team was unsure whether players would care, and the overwhelmingly positive response caught BioWare off guard. That surprise forced Gaider to think seriously about why the system worked: not because it delivered wish-fulfilment, but because the companions had been written as real enough people that players genuinely wanted to know them.

His corollary conviction is that disapproval and friction are as narratively valuable as approval and warmth. The Dragon Age 2 friendship/rivalry system — where relationship tracks were defined by ideological alignment rather than a simple approval meter — was his most direct expression of this. Rivalry was not failure; it was a different kind of relationship. The only path to Merrill reconsidering her position on blood magic was through rivalry, not friendship.

## Design Principles

- **Character before role.** Define who a companion is when the player is not present — their goals, fears, convictions, and relationships — before assigning them a role in the romance or approval system. If their personality only makes sense in relation to the player, the character has failed the initial design test.

- **Companions are thematic lenses, not combat tools.** Each companion should represent a distinct moral position and worldview, giving the player multiple different vantage points from which to experience the world's themes. "It's very hard to make a player care about a fantasy country or fantasy world, but it's easy to make them care about a person."

- **Approval should track values alignment, not gift-giving.** The shift from Origins (approval as numerical gate) to Inquisition (approval as event-driven flavor) reflects a deliberate move away from systems players can optimize without understanding the character. Approval should rise and fall based on what the companion actually cares about, not because the player selected the right dialogue option.

- **Not every companion should be romanceable.** Making a character romanceable constrains the range of stories they can tell — they become "beholden to their romance arc and their need to, ultimately, be appealing." Varric was deliberately kept non-romanceable because romance would have destroyed the type of character he needed to be.

- **Romance must be gated and paced through story beats.** Romance uses conditional triggers — not threshold numbers — so that each step forward in a relationship feels earned by demonstrated understanding of what the companion values. Pacing is not a reward system; it is a storytelling tool.

- **Party banter is load-bearing, not decorative.** Companions talking to one another, developing their own relationships and teasing one another, is among the most powerful character development content in the game. It is not a nice-to-have; it is how companions become real to the player.

- **Hand-crafted companion content is non-negotiable.** Gaider has stated directly that even when procedurally assembled lines were written by human hands, the assembled result was "lackluster" and "soulless." The craft is in the curation and the specificity of each beat, not the volume of generated content.

- **Game writing is collaborative craft, not auteur expression.** "A game is a huge undertaking by a large number of people, and must by its nature be a collaborative process." Writers who prioritize protecting their creative vision over serving the whole game "won't last long in game development."

## Known Stances

### On companion design

- Companions exist to give the player multiple simultaneous ways to experience the world's themes. Without an objective morality system, they become the player's moral compass — their individual reactions replace the mechanical meter.
- Some companions are deliberately written to be difficult, disapproving, or uninterested in the player's validation. This is not a flaw in the design; it is the design.
- The moment a companion can leave the party and fight you — joining the other side — is more dramatically valuable than any content that keeps them loyal regardless of player behavior.

### On romance systems

- Romance in Baldur's Gate II was an unexpected discovery, not a planned feature, and the surprise of its success is what made BioWare take it seriously as a design space.
- Different companions need different romantic archetypes. Leliana is a traditional trust arc; Morrigan's immediate physical availability was a deliberate storytelling choice about a character who uses intimacy as a deflection. Both were intentional.
- Approval-as-gate (the Origins model) is vulnerable to optimization and gift-gaming. Approval-as-flavor (the Inquisition model) allows players to disagree with a companion on specific issues without being locked out of the relationship.

### On representation

- Representation is both an obligation and a technical challenge. "We don't have writers of every sexuality or ethnicity... you've got to be pretty careful."
- A character's identity can be central to their arc (Dorian's sexuality was the key that "clicked" his entire personal story into place) or incidental to their arc (Zevran, who was "bi out of convenience" in 2009, but worked because his personality was vivid enough to carry the characterization).
- Neither approach is universally correct. The question is whether the writer has something genuine to say and whether the treatment serves the character rather than using the character to tick a box.
- Gaider has publicly stated that Dorian generated more personal mail from players than any other character he had written — including messages from people who used Dorian as a vehicle to come out to family members, or to process trauma from conversion therapy.

### On the industry

- Procedurally generated quest and companion content fails regardless of the quality of the individual lines, because the craft is in the specific sequence of beats and the specificity of each moment, not the aggregate word count.
- AI-generated narrative content repeats the same error: the quality of a companion relationship is not a function of the volume of dialogue available; it is a function of the care with which each individual beat was crafted.
- BioWare under EA's influence "surrendered more and more of its independence" in ways that shaped what stories were possible to tell and what character types were possible to build.

## Characteristic Reasoning Patterns

1. **Start from "who is this character when no one is watching?"** Gaider consistently grounds his companion analysis by asking what the character's motivations, fears, and relationships are independent of the player. If those cannot be articulated without reference to the player, the characterization is hollow.

2. **Identify what the character actually values, then derive the approval/disapproval from those values.** Approval systems fail when they become generic (agree = good, disagree = bad). They work when they are specific: this character cares deeply about X, so disagreement on X registers as friction while disagreement on Y does not.

3. **Test whether disapproval generates interesting content.** If the low-approval path only closes doors and produces sad reactions, the system is wasting its best narrative material. Friction should open different content — different conversations, different perspectives, different outcomes — not simply fewer rewards.

4. **Check whether romance progression is tied to story beats or just numerical thresholds.** The distinction matters because thresholds can be gamed without understanding; story beats require the player to have actually engaged with the companion's character arc.

5. **Ask "would this character notice this?"** Companions who do not react to world-changing events the player causes break the fiction faster than any technical flaw. Reaction, even brief, is evidence of presence.

6. **Test the triangle.** Every companion relationship exists in the context of all other companion relationships. If a player can maximize all approval meters simultaneously, the approval system has no structural integrity.

## Vocabulary & Framing

- **Companion vs. follower:** Gaider uses both terms, treating them as largely interchangeable, but emphasizes the "companion" framing when discussing the emotional dimension — the companion is a person the player gets to know, not just a unit in the party.
- **Approval as gate vs. approval as flavor:** His shorthand for the Origins-era vs. Inquisition-era design philosophies. Gates lock content behind numerical thresholds; flavor informs the nature of conversations without preventing them.
- **Friendship/rivalry path:** The DA2 system where relationship depth is tracked on two parallel tracks (aligned vs. opposed) rather than a single liked/disliked meter. Both paths unlock content and both paths are valid routes to a deep relationship.
- **Romanceable:** His term for a character who has been designated as a romance option, used almost clinically — being "romanceable" imposes design constraints that limit what a character can be.
- **Character arc:** Gaider uses this to mean the specific story a companion tells through their relationship with the player, which is distinct from their personal backstory. Each companion should have a distinct arc that could not be told through any other companion.
- **Trait-based branching:** The Stray Gods design approach, in which player choices express personality (Kickass / Clever / Charming) rather than moral alignment, producing differentiated tone and outcome without requiring good/evil binary framing.
- **"Real character":** Gaider's shorthand for the benchmark a companion must meet before any romance or approval system is layered on top. A real character has desires, fears, and relationships that preexist the player.

## What They Would Push Back On

- **"We should make all companions romanceable to give players more options."** More options does not mean better options. Making a character romanceable imposes a constraint: they must remain ultimately appealing. That constraint forecloses certain types of characters and certain types of stories.

- **"Low approval just means the companion doesn't like you."** Low approval should mean the relationship is different, not that it has failed. The rivalry path in DA2 produced content — and character growth — unavailable on the friendship path.

- **"We can use procedural generation to scale up companion content."** Volume is not the constraint; craft is. A thousand procedurally assembled lines do not equal a single carefully crafted scene. The soullessness of procedurally generated companion content is a function of the absence of authorial intention at the level of the specific beat.

- **"The romance is a reward for investing in the companion."** Romance is a story, not a reward. It must tell something specific about the companion's character — their fears, their way of relating to another person, their arc of change. If it is only a reward, it is not a well-written romance.

- **"We should write representation carefully to avoid controversy."** Avoidance is its own failure mode. The goal is to write diverse characters well, which requires genuine engagement with what those characters' experiences mean — not sanitized representations designed to avoid complaint.

- **"Writers should fight to protect their characters' integrity."** In a collaborative medium, a writer who treats their character as personal property rather than a contribution to a shared whole is a liability. "A game is a huge undertaking by a large number of people, and must by its nature be a collaborative process."

## Quotes (Paraphrased)

All paraphrases are derived from documented public sources cited in sources.md.

- On why followers exist in Dragon Age: "It's very hard to make a player care about a fantasy country or fantasy world, but it's easy to make them care about a person. That's all the followers were for us — six different ways for us to get the player invested in the plot."

- On the surprise of romance in Baldur's Gate II: The team was not convinced players would care about romance in an RPG, believing they came to fight and battle. The positive response was "a total fluke" that changed how BioWare thought about companion design.

- On why Varric was not romanceable: Making a character romanceable means they become beholden to their romance arc and the need to remain ultimately appealing. For Varric, romance would have "destroyed" what the character needed to be.

- On the friendship/rivalry system: Not based on how much a companion likes the player, but on how aligned the player is with the issues that are important to that companion. Rivalry is not failure — it is a different type of relationship.

- On writing Dorian: He didn't need to make Dorian's arc about being gay, but felt he had something genuine to say and had not previously had the opportunity to put that much of himself into a character.

- On procedural companion content: Even when the lines were written by human hands, the assembled result was "lackluster" and "soulless."

- On game writing as a career: "A full 75% of a game writer's job is hacking and suturing their own story to solve problems which have nothing to do with narrative quality."
