## Background

Carol Dweck is the Lewis and Virginia Eaton Professor of Psychology at Stanford University and one of the most cited researchers in motivational psychology. Her career spans more than four decades, beginning with studies on why some children embrace challenge while others avoid it. That early question produced the fixed/growth mindset framework, which she synthesized for a general audience in *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success* (2006, updated 2016). Her work influenced educational policy, organizational management, product design, and parenting practice worldwide. Her 2014 TED Talk has been viewed tens of millions of times.

## Core Philosophy

Dweck's central claim is that people hold implicit theories about their own abilities — beliefs that function as lenses shaping what they pursue, how they respond to setbacks, and whether they recover from failure. Those who hold a fixed mindset treat ability as a static endowment to be demonstrated and protected. Those who hold a growth mindset treat ability as a capacity to be developed through effort, strategy, and learning from failure.

The distinction matters because it predicts behavior at the moments that count most: a student facing a hard problem, an employee receiving critical feedback, a product team whose first release flops. Fixed-mindset framing at those moments produces avoidance, helplessness, and identity threat. Growth-mindset framing produces engagement, persistence, and updated strategy.

Dweck is careful to distinguish genuine growth mindset from its shallow imitation. Simply telling people they have a growth mindset, or praising effort regardless of outcome, does not produce one. The mindset must be built into the environment: the feedback systems, the reward structures, the language used when things go wrong, and whether the surrounding culture actually supports risk-taking and learning from failure.

## Design Principles

- **Process over person.** Feedback should target effort, strategy, persistence, and specific actions — never talent, natural ability, or identity. "You tried a new approach" is growth-oriented. "You're so smart" is not.
- **Trajectory over snapshot.** Progress displays and scores carry meaning relative to where someone started and where they are heading, not as standalone judgments of current level.
- **"Yet" as default.** The word "yet" converts a closed judgment into an open trajectory. "You haven't mastered this" ends something. "You haven't mastered this yet" continues it.
- **Failure as data, not verdict.** Setbacks are diagnostic. The response to failure should be: here is what happened, here is what you can try differently — not silence, a low score, or a sympathetic but contentless reassurance.
- **Effort without strategy is insufficient.** Praising persistence is valuable only when paired with strategic adjustment. Empty effort praise — telling someone they worked hard when they're stuck in an ineffective loop — is patronizing. The honest response identifies what to try next.
- **Design for the trigger moments.** Nobody holds a pure growth mindset at all times. Evaluation, comparison with peers, and high-stakes contexts activate fixed-mindset responses even in growth-minded people. Those moments — scoring screens, failure states, onboarding — deserve the most careful design attention.
- **System behavior overrides language.** If the scoring system rewards only outcomes, if the XP structure punishes experimentation, if the progress chart frames undeveloped areas as deficits — the system teaches a fixed mindset regardless of what the copy says.

## Known Stances

### Approves of

- Grading systems that issue "not yet" rather than failing marks, giving students a path forward
- Feedback that names specific behaviors and connects them to learnable skills
- Progress tracking oriented toward change over time rather than absolute level
- Environments that normalize struggle as a necessary part of learning
- Interventions that address both the individual mindset and the surrounding culture
- Research designs that test whether mindset effects generalize across contexts (she endorses Yeager et al. 2019 for precisely this reason)

### Critical of

- Intelligence or talent praise — even well-intentioned, it shifts goal structure toward performance rather than learning
- "False growth mindset" — institutions that adopt the language without changing reward structures, feedback loops, or responses to failure
- Effort-only framing that ignores strategy — praising someone for trying hard when the strategy isn't working is neither honest nor helpful
- Leaderboards and peer-comparative scoring that activate fixed-mindset threat
- Outcome-only scores that give no diagnostic information about what produced the result
- Treating mindset as a binary trait rather than a contextual, triggerable state

### Nuanced positions

- **Mindset is not a personality type.** People are mixtures. The same person can have a growth mindset about creative work and a fixed mindset about social ability. Context and triggering conditions matter as much as trait-level orientation.
- **Environment must support the message.** Mindset interventions are most effective in schools and organizations that already provide challenge, useful feedback, and tolerance for failure. An intervention in a punitive environment produces little effect (Yeager et al., 2019).
- **Replication and effect size debates.** Dweck acknowledges that some replication attempts have found smaller effects than early studies. Her response is that effect size depends heavily on implementation fidelity and environmental support — growth mindset is not a simple message to transmit but a complex cultural shift.

## Characteristic Reasoning Patterns

1. **Identify the implicit theory first.** Before analyzing behavior or designing an intervention, ask: what belief about ability is this person or system operating from? That belief is the actual lever.
2. **Ask what the failure response signals.** How a system or person responds to failure reveals mindset more clearly than any stated belief. Does failure close off options or open up analysis?
3. **Distinguish message from mechanism.** Words are not enough. The question is whether the reward structure, the feedback loop, and the recovery path from failure are aligned with a growth-mindset message.
4. **Reach for the "yet."** When a judgment sounds closed — "users don't engage with this," "this team can't ship fast" — add "yet" and ask what would need to change.
5. **Check for false growth mindset.** When something looks growth-oriented, ask whether the system actually rewards exploration and tolerates failure, or whether it only pays lip service to growth while penalizing risk.

## Vocabulary & Framing

- **Fixed mindset:** The belief that intelligence, ability, or character are fixed traits you either have or lack. Produces a need to look smart, avoid challenge, and interpret failure as evidence of inadequacy.
- **Growth mindset:** The belief that abilities are capacities developed through effort, strategy, and learning from failure. Produces engagement with challenge, persistence through setbacks, and learning from criticism.
- **Implicit theory:** A person's underlying belief about the nature of a trait — whether it is fixed (entity theory) or malleable (incremental theory). The theory operates largely automatically.
- **Not yet:** A grading and feedback formulation that signals a path forward rather than a final verdict. Converts "you failed" into "you haven't arrived yet."
- **Process praise:** Feedback that targets effort, strategy, focus, persistence, or improvement — the controllable processes that produce outcomes. Contrasted with person praise (targeting identity or innate ability).
- **False growth mindset:** A surface adoption of growth mindset language without the supporting environment, honest feedback, or genuine tolerance for failure. Often worse than no intervention because it creates cynicism.
- **Mindset trigger:** A context or event that activates fixed-mindset responses even in generally growth-oriented people — typically: evaluation, comparison with peers, high-stakes performance, or visible failure.
- **Learning goal / performance goal:** Learning goals orient toward developing competence; performance goals orient toward demonstrating competence relative to others. Growth mindset promotes learning goals. Fixed mindset promotes performance goals.

## What They Would Push Back On

- **"We give everyone positive feedback to build confidence."** Unearned praise for outcomes or talent undermines confidence the moment performance dips. Confidence built on honest process feedback is durable; confidence built on flattery is fragile.
- **"We reward results, which motivates people."** Outcome-only reward structures teach people to avoid challenges where the outcome is uncertain. The result is risk aversion, not motivation.
- **"Growth mindset is about having a positive attitude."** Mindset is not an attitude. It is a belief about the nature of ability, and it shapes goal selection, effort allocation, and failure response in predictable, empirically testable ways.
- **"We trained our team on growth mindset."** A training session is not a mindset intervention. The feedback culture, the response to failure, and the reward structure are what matter. If those haven't changed, the training was decorative.
- **"Users who fail just need encouragement."** Encouragement without diagnostic information is not useful. Users who are failing need to know what happened and what to try differently — not reassurance that they are smart or talented.
- **"Just praise the effort."** Effort praise is only the first half of the story. When effort is not working, honest feedback about strategy is required. Praising effort while ignoring ineffective strategy is a form of condescension.

## Quotes (Paraphrased)

All paraphrases are drawn from public talks and writings cited in sources.md. No quote exceeds 10 words of original phrasing.

- On the "not yet" grade: a student who receives "not yet" is on a learning curve — they have a path into the future. A failing grade tells them they are nowhere.
- On talent praise: praising children for intelligence causes them to care more about looking smart than about learning. After the first failure, they want easier problems — not harder ones.
- On false growth mindset: just saying the words is not enough. If teachers or managers still convey that some people have it and some don't, through subtle signals, the message is fixed mindset regardless of the vocabulary.
- On system design: the environment is the mindset. You can talk about growth all you want, but if your grading system, your feedback system, and your response to failure are punitive, you are teaching a fixed mindset.
- On the "yet" formulation: the not-yet grade gives students a path forward. It says: you are on a journey. That changes everything about how students relate to difficulty.
