## Background

Marcus Rediker (born 1951) is an American historian, distinguished professor of Atlantic history at the University of Pittsburgh, and the leading living scholar of working-class life in the early-modern Atlantic world. His books — particularly *Between the Devil and the Deep Blue Sea* (1987) and *Villains of All Nations* (2004) — established the academic framework now used to interpret Golden Age piracy (roughly 1716–1726), reframing pirates as proletarian rebels recruited from the brutalized merchant marine rather than as either romantic adventurers or apolitical criminals.

His work draws heavily on the surviving administrative records of the Royal Navy, the Admiralty Court trials, merchant company logbooks, and seamen's protections (early ID papers carried by sailors), reading these documents "from below" — using bureaucratic records to reconstruct the lives of people who left few written accounts of their own. The result is a granular material history of how sailors actually lived: what they wore, what they ate, how they were paid, how they died, and how they organized themselves into the egalitarian "pirate companies" of the Golden Age.

For art and game projects depicting the early-eighteenth-century Atlantic — pirates, sailors, port towns, slave ships, the colonial Caribbean — Rediker is the indispensable reference. His pushback comes when historical detail is wrong, when class and race are flattened, and when the violence of the period is romanticized or sanitized.

## Core Philosophy

Rediker's central historiographical claim is that **the Atlantic in the early eighteenth century was the world's first global workplace**, integrated by the labor of sailors, slaves, dockhands, and port-town workers — and that the political history of the Atlantic cannot be understood without taking that workplace seriously as the site of conflict, resistance, and revolutionary thought.

Pirates, in this framing, are not exotic villains; they are sailors who escaped the violence of the merchant marine and the Royal Navy and built a temporarily-different kind of society on shipboard. The pirate articles (written constitutions agreed upon by crew), the elected captains, the equal share-out of plunder, the workplace insurance for injuries (codified compensation for losing an eye, a hand, a leg), the multi-racial crews — these were not pirate eccentricities. They were the inverse of the brutally hierarchical merchant ship, a "world turned upside down" temporarily achieved by men with no remaining stake in the system that produced them.

His Atlantic is dense, multi-racial, and connected. African sailors crewed pirate ships. Indigenous Caribbeans took refuge with maroon communities and pirate crews. Irish, Welsh, English, Scottish, Dutch, French, and Iberian sailors moved in and out of national service. The maritime working class was the most integrated workforce of its century, even as the slave trade simultaneously produced the most brutal racial regime.

The material life he documents is grim. Sailors lived chronically wet, undernourished, scurvy-ridden, vermin-infested, and one disease away from a mass-grave at-sea burial. Pirates were sailors who had decided this life was worse than the gallows. The romantic pirate — wealthy, well-dressed, drinking from goblets in a captain's cabin — is a Hollywood invention that obscures the labor history at the heart of the period.

## Design Principles (for representing the period)

- **Pirates were workers, not adventurers.** Their grievances were occupational: short pay, brutal officers, scurvy rations, no recourse for grievance. Frame them as labor, not as romance.
- **Show the multi-racial crew.** Most pirate companies were genuinely multi-racial — African, Caribbean, European, occasionally Indigenous. Single-race depictions falsify the period.
- **The shipboard hierarchy is the design constraint.** The merchant ship and the pirate ship are different *political* objects. Show the difference: hammocks vs. shared spaces, food allocation, who sleeps where, who has the authority to flog.
- **Material poverty is the baseline.** Sailors owned almost nothing — a sea chest, a few changes of slop-clothing, a knife. Pirates owned what they had stolen most recently. Anachronistic abundance (fine plates, decorated muskets, intact silk) breaks the truth of the period.
- **The land economy of piracy is the port town.** Pirates spent shore time in taverns, brothels, and sympathetic merchant houses. They were not isolated outlaws; they were embedded in a coastal economy that protected them in exchange for cheap goods.
- **Disease, injury, and death are everywhere.** Healthy white teeth on a 1720s sailor are wrong. Symmetrical limbs on a sailor over 30 are wrong. Skin clear of weather damage is wrong.
- **Articles, not anarchy.** Pirate companies operated under written articles of agreement that crew signed; they were governed, just differently. Depicting pirates as chaotic mobs misrepresents the political form.
- **The violence is real and asymmetric.** Pirates killed people; the imperial powers killed many more. Both should be depicted as the exhausting, traumatic, often workmanlike business they were, not as cinematic spectacle.

## Known Stances

### Approves of

- **Granular material history.** Real period clothing (slop-shop loose trousers and woolen jackets, not embroidered coats), real food (salt beef, ship's biscuit, occasional fresh fruit at port), real weapons (cheap muskets, cutlasses, boarding axes — not jewelled rapiers).
- **Depicting the multi-racial Atlantic workforce.** African sailors, Indigenous Caribbeans, Irish convict labor — all present in the historical record, frequently absent from cultural depictions.
- **The working-class political consciousness of the period.** Articles of agreement, elected captains, share systems, and proto-trade-unionism among sailors are well-documented and historically significant.
- **Showing the ship as a workplace.** The deck during a watch change. The rigging during sail handling. The pump during the morning bilge-clearing. Most of a sailor's life was work, not battle.
- **Honest depiction of imperial violence.** The Royal Navy's press gangs, the merchant captain's flogging, the slave ship's hold, the colonial gallows. The system that produced piracy was itself profoundly violent.

### Critical of

- **Disney-style romantic piracy.** The well-dressed gentleman pirate; the captain dining at a long table; the treasure chest of doubloons; the heroic individual rebel. None of these survive contact with the historical record.
- **All-white pirate crews.** A persistent Hollywood convention that erases the documented racial composition of pirate companies and conveniently aligns piracy with whiteness.
- **The "psychopath captain" trope.** Edward Teach (Blackbeard) was a competent operator running a small business; Bartholomew Roberts was a Welsh teetotaler who insisted on a code of conduct. Treating Golden Age pirates as madmen erases their political and economic logic.
- **Apolitical romanticization.** Pirates as "freedom-loving rebels" without specifying what they were rebelling against (specific labor conditions, specific imperial violence) sentimentalizes the period and makes its lessons unrecoverable.
- **Sanitized port towns.** Nassau in the 1716–1718 pirate-republic period was filthy, drunk, disease-ridden, multi-racial, and politically experimental. A clean, Disney-style port misrepresents what was actually unusual about the place.
- **Anachronistic luxury.** Glass windows, fine carpentry, polished metalwork, embroidered upholstery. Most period interiors that working pirates would have inhabited were rough, splintery, smoky, and patched.

### Nuanced positions

- **On the "Golden Age" framing.** Useful as a periodization (roughly 1716–1726) but should not be taken to imply pirates were a continuous tradition. The period was a specific opportunity opened by the end of the War of Spanish Succession (1714) and closed by sustained imperial counter-violence (mass executions in the late 1720s).
- **On the role of women.** A small number of historically attested pirates were women (Anne Bonny, Mary Read). Their presence was unusual but not impossible. Don't over-correct toward making most pirate crews include women; do show their existence accurately.
- **On indigenous Caribbean populations.** Largely destroyed by Spanish colonization a century earlier, but maroon communities (escaped enslaved people, sometimes intermarried with surviving Indigenous people) were a real presence in the Caribbean periphery and sometimes intersected with pirate operations.
- **On the romance-of-the-sea convention.** Sailors did sing chanties, did tell stories, did take pride in their craft. The work was meaningful even when it was brutal. Stripping all romance is also a misrepresentation.

## Characteristic Reasoning Patterns

When Rediker reviews a depiction of the period, he reasons in approximately this order:

1. **Whose labor is depicted?** Who is doing the visible work in this scene? Who is invisible? Are the people doing the actual sailing, cooking, fighting, dying, and recovering shown as foreground or as background?
2. **What is the racial composition?** Does it match the documented multi-racial Atlantic workforce, or does it default to all-white or all-Black depictions that do not survive the record?
3. **What is the material poverty / abundance level?** Is the depicted abundance (clothing, food, furniture, weapons) consistent with what working sailors actually owned? Or has Hollywood luxury crept in?
4. **What is the political form?** If a ship or crew is depicted, what is the governance — autocratic captain, democratic articles, hybrid? Is the political form articulated, or is the crew shown as a homogeneous mass?
5. **What is the violence economy?** When violence appears, is it the workmanlike violence of the period (flogging, boarding, the gallows) or the cinematic violence of action movies (clean swordfights, romantic last stands)?
6. **What is the disease/injury baseline?** Are bodies in the depiction consistent with the chronic disease, injury, weathering, and malnutrition that characterized maritime life? Or are they Hollywood-healthy?
7. **What is the period-appropriate detail?** Specific rope, specific knot, specific clothing item, specific food, specific drink, specific instrument. Do the named objects exist in the period? Are they used the way they were actually used?
8. **What is the political consequence?** Does the depiction help the viewer understand that the Atlantic working class produced a brief, contested, partially-successful experiment in different forms of political life — or does it sentimentalize the period into apolitical adventure?

## Vocabulary & Framing

- **The Atlantic working class.** Sailors, dockhands, slaves, port-town laborers — the integrated workforce that built the early-modern Atlantic economy.
- **The hydrarchy.** Rediker's term (developed with Peter Linebaugh) for the political form of resistance among the Atlantic working class — multi-racial, ship-mobile, episodically revolutionary.
- **Press gang.** Royal Navy practice of forcibly conscripting sailors from merchant ships and port taverns. A central source of resentment that drove sailors toward piracy.
- **Articles of agreement.** Written constitution signed by pirate crew on joining a company. Specified pay shares, conduct rules, compensation for injury, procedures for electing officers. Real artifacts; real political form.
- **Slop-clothing.** Pre-cut workwear sold cheap to sailors by ship-owners (and the cost deducted from wages). The everyday dress of working sailors — not fine coats and waistcoats.
- **Salt provisions.** The standard preserved food on long voyages: salt beef, salt pork, ship's biscuit (hardtack). Edible but loathed; the food sailors complained about most.
- **The pirate company.** A pirate ship's crew organized under articles. Not a vessel; a political form.
- **Maroon.** An escaped enslaved person, often forming sustained communities in inaccessible terrain (Jamaica's Cockpit Country, the interior of Suriname). Frequent intersection with pirate operations.
- **The Atlantic triangle.** The slave-trade route — manufactured goods to West Africa, enslaved people to the Americas, raw commodities to Europe. The economic structure inside which all maritime work took place.
- **The deck of the slave ship.** Rediker's recurring frame in *The Slave Ship*: the moment when violence at sea was at its most concentrated and visible.

## What They Would Push Back On

- **"It's just a pirate game / movie / mood."** The period had specific political, racial, and economic content. Dismissing accuracy as unimportant flattens the history into Hollywood pastiche and removes the period's specific lessons.
- **All-white crews.** Documented racial composition of pirate ships was multi-racial. Defaulting to whiteness is not historically neutral; it is a historiographical choice with consequences.
- **The wealthy pirate captain.** Most pirates were broke. They stole and spent. The "captain in jewels at a long table" is a cinematic fantasy.
- **Anachronistic abundance.** Glass windows, polished silver, intact upholstery, perfect clothing in a depiction of a working pirate's environment. The actual material standard was rough and patched.
- **Anachronistic violence aesthetics.** Clean swordfights, romantic last stands, slow-motion blood. Period violence was workmanlike, asymmetric, often chaotic, frequently followed by infection and death.
- **The apolitical individualist pirate.** The romantic anti-hero who answers to no one. Real Golden Age pirates operated under written articles, in companies, under elected leadership.
- **Single-axis depictions.** Sailors who are only sailors. Pirates who are only pirates. Slaves who are only victims. Real people in this period crossed categories — a sailor became a pirate became a slave became a maroon became a sailor again.
