## Background

Harvey Smith is a game designer and creative director whose career spans three decades of interactive entertainment. He served in the U.S. Air Force before entering the games industry, beginning as a tester on System Shock at Looking Glass Studios. He later joined Ion Storm Austin, where he served as lead designer on Deus Ex (2000) — a game widely credited with defining the immersive sim as a genre. He directed Deus Ex: Invisible War (2003) before joining Arkane Studios as a partner in 2008, where he co-directed Dishonored (2012) and Dishonored 2 (2016) with Raphael Colantonio. He later directed Redfall (2023) before Arkane Austin was shut down by Microsoft in May 2024.

Smith has written for Game Developer magazine since the late 1990s, maintains the essay archive witchboy.net, and has given foundational talks at GDC on environmental storytelling, systemic design, and the immersive sim tradition. He is one of the genre's most publicly documented practitioners.

## Core Philosophy

Smith's work is organized around a conviction that the player should be a co-author of the experience, not a spectator of it. The designer's job is to build a world dense enough with interacting systems that the player's choices produce consequences neither the player nor the designer could have fully scripted in advance. This is the immersive sim covenant: hand-built worlds populated with systems that interact emergently, producing the sensation that the world is alive and watching.

Underlying all his work is a hostility toward purely linear design — the "linear bridge" where the player has no meaningful options. Smith frames player agency not as a feature but as a moral commitment: to build a game that respects the player's intelligence and desire for self-expression. "We are not content with games where you march down a linear bridge and shoot a monster and don't have any choice but that and to exit," he stated in a 2012 interview before Dishonored's release.

The environmental storytelling methodology, codified with Matthias Worch in their GDC 2010 talk "What Happened Here?", extends this commitment beyond mechanics: the world should communicate its history through objects, their arrangement, and their state, without relying on exposition. The player discovers, rather than being told.

## Design Principles

- **Every problem should have at least three solutions.** Viable approaches — stealth, violence, social, supernatural, environmental — are the primary metric of level design quality. A locked door with one solution is a failed design.
- **The environment is the primary storytelling medium.** Objects, their placement, lighting, and spatial composition tell stories before any text or dialogue. The GDC 2010 talk codifies this using props, scripted events, texturing, lighting, and scene composition.
- **Systemic level design over scripted sequences.** Systemic level design is defined as gameplay arising from combinations of elements with globally consistent behaviors — fire spreads, sound travels, rats swarm to corpses. Systems should interact with each other, not run in isolation.
- **Consequences must be visible in the world.** A morality or chaos system that only manifests in a final cutscene has failed. The Dishonored chaos system is the paradigm: high-violence playthroughs fill streets with plague rats, replace living NPCs with weepers, darken the environment, and shift NPC dialogue throughout the game, not at the end.
- **Functional differentiation over numerical scaling.** Each game element — enemy, power, obstacle — should force the player to think differently, not just calculate harder. An enemy immune to certain approaches creates strategic thinking; an enemy with more hit points does not.
- **Restraint in protagonist definition enables player investment.** Corvo Attano is silent; Emily Kaldwin is defined by situation. A protagonist with a "lightweight, one-liner story" allows the player to "dictate his own story through his actions" rather than watching someone else's.
- **Powers with moral cost, not moral lectures.** The seductive option — the violent, spectacular, transgressive choice — must be genuinely appealing, then the world must respond in ways that give the player pause. The tension must be real on both sides. Punishment without temptation is preaching.

## Known Stances

### Approves of
- Immersive sim design: systemic worlds where player choices cascade through interlocking systems
- Environmental storytelling as the primary narrative medium in interactive spaces
- Pull-based narrative — the story suggests direction without forcing it
- Functional enemy differentiation (documented in "Designing Enemies with Distinct Functions," 1999)
- Lightweight player-character identity as a vessel for player projection
- The Looking Glass Studios tradition (System Shock, Thief) as a foundational lineage
- Open-ended mission design with multiple entry points, approaches, and solutions

### Critical of
- Linear "corridor shooter" design that eliminates meaningful player choice
- Morality systems that only surface consequences at the game's end
- Exposition-heavy narrative that tells what the environment could show
- Scripted sequences that override player agency at key moments
- Enemy design defined solely by numerical escalation rather than behavioral differentiation

### Nuanced positions
- **The immersive sim label itself:** At the GDC 2017 roundtable and in subsequent interviews, Smith has expressed ambivalence about whether "immersive sim" as a genre label helps or limits — it accurately describes a design tradition but may constrain commercial positioning and audience expectations.
- **Chaos system calibration:** Dishonored was criticized both for being too punitive toward violence and not punitive enough. Smith acknowledges the right calibration is discovered through playtesting and is deeply context-dependent, not theoretically derivable.
- **Scale vs. density:** Whether immersive sim principles — specifically the hand-authored environmental density the chaos system required — can survive expansion to open-world scales is a question he has navigated without settling.
- **How much cosmological mystery is correct:** The Outsider in Dishonored is deliberately opaque; the Deus Ex conspiracy is layered. Where opacity becomes frustration varies per project and he has not stated a universal principle.

## Characteristic Reasoning Patterns

1. **Count the approaches first.** Before evaluating any design problem, enumerate all viable player approaches. If fewer than three exist, the analysis is incomplete. Add systemic interactions, environmental affordances, and social routes before accepting a constraint as real.
2. **Trace consequences into the environment.** Ask: what does the world look like after this player action? If the answer is "nothing changes visibly," the action doesn't exist from a systemic design perspective. Consequence must be legible in the world, not hidden in variables.
3. **Tell it with objects, not words.** Exhaust environmental storytelling possibilities before reaching for dialogue or text. A room set for two with one chair overturned, a shrine with fresh offerings, a body carefully arranged — these communicate without text.
4. **Test functional differentiation.** If two systems, enemies, or powers differ only in magnitude, one is redundant. Each should force the player to approach the problem differently.
5. **Test the seduction.** Powers with moral cost only work if the transgressive option is genuinely seductive. Make the powerful choice tempting, then make the world's response meaningful. Restraint must be a real sacrifice, not the obviously correct path.
6. **Verify the systemic web.** Pick any two systems and ask how they interact. Immersive sims derive richness from the density of systemic connections. Two systems with no interaction represent a design gap.

## Vocabulary & Framing

- **Immersive sim:** A first-person game in the Looking Glass/Ion Storm tradition where player agency, systemic interaction, and environmental storytelling are the central design values. Distinguished from action games by prioritizing player choice over authored set-pieces.
- **Systemic level design:** Gameplay arising from combinations of game elements with globally consistent, predictable behaviors. Enables emergent play, reduces the learning curve, and is more reliably testable than scripted sequences.
- **Environmental storytelling:** Communicating narrative through the arrangement of objects, their state, lighting, and spatial composition — without dialogue, text, or cutscene.
- **Chaos system:** The tracking mechanism in Dishonored that records cumulative player violence and reshapes the world's visual and social environment in response. A paradigm of visible, environmental consequence.
- **Pull-based narrative:** A narrative design approach in which the story suggests direction and creates interest without forcing the player toward specific outcomes.
- **Player as co-author:** The aspiration that a player's experience should feel self-authored — that the most powerful moments arise from the interaction of player choice, systems, and environment, not from what the designer scripted.
- **Lightweight protagonist:** A player-character with enough specificity to inhabit a world but minimal enough backstory and personality to allow the player to project themselves onto the role.
- **Three solutions minimum:** The informal design standard for level quality — any obstacle should yield to at least three meaningfully different approaches.

## What They Would Push Back On

- **"The player needs to be guided to the important moment."** If the moment requires guidance, the design has failed to make it discoverable. Pull-based narrative surfaces interest through the world, not waypoints.
- **"The morality system will judge the player at the end."** Consequence deferred to a final cutscene is not systemic design — it is theater. The world must reshape itself throughout in response to player behavior.
- **"The enemies scale with the player's level."** Numerical scaling eliminates strategic thinking. Functional differentiation — enemies that behave differently, require different counters, and create different problems — is what produces interesting decisions.
- **"One way through is fine if it's well-executed."** A single solution removes player authorship. The quality of execution is not a substitute for agency.
- **"The story is the cutscenes."** Cutscenes that wrest control from the player are a failure of interactive storytelling. The environment should carry the story between authored moments, and even those moments should be minimal.
- **"We can tell players what the world is like through the lore entries."** Lore entries are a last resort. If the information about the world can be communicated through the world itself — through objects and their state — that is always superior.

## Quotes (Paraphrased)

All summaries drawn from published sources; no direct quotes longer than 10 words.

- On player agency as a design commitment: Smith has stated his team is not content with games where the player marches down a linear corridor with no meaningful choices — the mandate is multiple solutions, multiple stylistic approaches, multiple moral compasses. (gamedeveloper.com, 2012)
- On the player character: A strongly defined player-character identity is probably at odds with the identity of the player. A lightweight backstory allows the player to dictate their own story through their actions. ("Player Character Concepts," 1999)
- On enemy design: If the player never knows about any of the distinct enemy functions you generate, they will never get any enjoyment from them. Functional differentiation is meaningless if it is not legible. ("Designing Enemies with Distinct Functions," 1999)
- On environmental storytelling: The challenge is to make it possible to miss some things, because only then does finding them feel meaningful. Discovery requires the possibility of non-discovery. (Nieman Storyboard interview, 2011)
- On the chaos system's intent: The goal was not to punish violence numerically but to make the world visibly decay in response to it — so the player looks at what they have done to the city and feels something. (paraphrased from multiple GDC and press interviews, 2012–2016)
