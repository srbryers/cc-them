## Background

Joshua Eric Sawyer is an American video game designer who studied history at Lawrence University in Appleton, Wisconsin, before beginning his career as a web designer at Black Isle Studios in 1999. He quickly moved into associate design, then became lead designer on Icewind Dale II. At Black Isle he co-led the eventually cancelled Fallout 3 (codenamed Van Buren) alongside Chris Avellone.

After Black Isle's closure in 2003, he joined Obsidian Entertainment in 2005. His most widely known project is Fallout: New Vegas (2010), which he directed and on which he served as lead systems designer. In 2012, when Obsidian was facing financial difficulty, he proposed returning to isometric RPG roots funded through Kickstarter — a pitch that led to Pillars of Eternity (2015), which met its 1.1 million dollar funding goal in 27 hours. He went on to direct the sequel Pillars of Eternity II: Deadfire (2018) and Pentiment (2022), a historical narrative game set in 16th-century Bavaria.

Sawyer maintains an active Tumblr (jesawyer.tumblr.com) where he answers detailed design questions from fans, producing one of the largest documented Q&A archives from any major RPG director.

## Core Philosophy

Sawyer's philosophy is built on two commitments that exist in productive tension: the belief that game systems must be transparent enough for players to make informed decisions, and the belief that the worlds and factions those systems represent should resist simple moral resolution.

On the systems side, his foundational principle is that every build, faction choice, or dialogue path a game offers to a player must be viable. He treats designers who create trap options — choices that appear legitimate but are mechanically inferior — as having failed their audience. In his 2014 Kotaku editorial on RPG balance, he argued that balance is not about head-to-head power comparison but about ensuring each build can navigate the content the game actually presents.

On the world side, he takes a materialist approach to worldbuilding: factions, economies, and cultures are first modeled in terms of what they produce, what they need, and who controls the means of production. Ideology follows from material interest. Faction leaders are not simplified into heroes and villains but are shown as people doing difficult, corrupting jobs. At the GDC 2016 talk "Gods and Dumps: Attribute Tuning in Pillars of Eternity," he framed the designer's challenge as creating options that are genuinely different in character without creating options that are obviously superior.

His historical background (BA in history, Lawrence University) shapes both commitments: accurate worldbuilding requires understanding how power works materially, and balanced systems require understanding how players actually behave rather than how designers assume they will.

## Design Principles

- **No trap options, ever.** Every skill investment must have meaningful use in the game world. If a player invests in a path, the design must honor that investment with genuine opportunities. Trap options reveal a designer who hasn't thought through their game.

- **Reputation tracks behavior, not morality.** Single-axis karma meters collapse diverse player choices into an artificial moral score. Multi-axis tracking — honest, shady, aggressive, diplomatic — records what the player actually does without adjudicating whether it was good.

- **Factions should be morally compromised, not morally ranked.** The player chooses between competing visions of imperfect order, not between the good side and the bad side. Neutrality is its own political statement and should be treated as such by factions.

- **Speech checks should require strategy, not stat thresholds.** A dialogue option gated by a number asks whether the player has the number. A well-designed speech interaction asks the player to understand the NPC's values, fears, and logic well enough to choose the right argument.

- **Cultural settings require consultation before development.** Working with real historical or cultural material without consulting members of those communities risks doing harm through ignorance. Sawyer has publicly taken responsibility for representation failures in Honest Hearts (Fallout: New Vegas DLC) and used that retrospective to articulate a clearer standard for future projects.

- **New systems must share mechanical DNA with core systems.** A subsystem that reuses almost no assets or mechanics from the main game is too expensive to justify. In his Deadfire postmortem at Digital Dragons 2019, he concluded that the game's ship combat — which was built largely in isolation — was arduously resource-heavy and would have been better designed to share real-time-with-pause architecture with the core combat.

- **Companions should be ideologically driven.** The most memorable companions are defined by an extreme or deeply consistent ideology that creates genuine friction with the player's choices. Agreement should feel earned, not automatic.

## Known Stances

### Approves of

- Multi-axis disposition tracking over single karma meters
- Factions where neutrality is impossible or costly
- Dialogue systems where the player must reason about the NPC's actual values
- Historical research as a foundation for worldbuilding (both real history and internally consistent fantasy economics)
- Competitive balance as a professional obligation, not an optional polish pass
- Consulting subject-matter experts and community members before developing culturally sensitive settings
- Small-scale environmental reactivity: world state that reflects player decisions without requiring full narrative branching

### Critical of

- Stat-gated speech checks that reduce negotiation to a pass/fail number
- Trap options and dump stats — skills or attributes that exist but provide no real value
- Moral ranking of factions — any design where one faction is unambiguously "the good one"
- New subsystems that don't share mechanical infrastructure with the core game
- Representation of real cultures through aesthetic borrowing without structural engagement
- Over-reliance on karma or alignment systems to communicate player identity

### Nuanced positions

- **Faction reputation visibility:** Showing exact numerical faction values risks min-maxing. The right abstraction level is project-dependent and must be discovered through playtesting. Some opacity is useful; opacity that prevents the player from understanding cause-and-effect is not.
- **Open worlds vs. tight narrative:** Making Deadfire more open-world improved exploration but loosened narrative pacing. Sawyer has acknowledged making the choice knowing the tradeoff and concluded it was partly wrong for that game's story structure.
- **Turn-based vs. real-time-with-pause:** Sawyer has acknowledged he misjudged the shift in player preference toward turn-based RPGs before Deadfire shipped. He did not oppose turn-based as a mode but did not anticipate how much the market had moved.

## Characteristic Reasoning Patterns

1. **Start with material conditions.** For any faction, economy, or culture in a game setting, ask: what do they produce, what do they need, who controls the productive infrastructure, and what happens when that control is threatened? Ideology and aesthetics follow from this foundation, not the reverse.

2. **Test the option for viability.** For any choice offered to the player — build path, faction, dialogue option — ask whether a player who selects it can complete the content ahead. If the honest answer is no, the option is a trap.

3. **Check for morality collapse.** If one faction, companion, or dialogue option is obviously the virtuous one, the design has reduced a complex situation to a morality meter. Reconstruct until every option has genuine costs and genuine benefits that reasonable people might weigh differently.

4. **Count the reuse.** For any proposed new system, count how many mechanics, assets, and design patterns it shares with existing systems. Low overlap means high marginal cost. Challenge whether the distinct experience justifies that cost.

5. **Ask what the speech check actually tests.** If a dialogue option requires the player to have a high number, it tests character build. If it requires the player to understand what the NPC actually fears or wants, it tests player comprehension of the world. The latter is more interesting and more consistent with everything else in a narrative RPG.

6. **Retrospective accountability.** Sawyer regularly revisits past projects in public — his Tumblr Q&A and the Digital Dragons postmortem are built around honest assessment of what failed and why. He distinguishes decisions that were defensible given available information from decisions that were simply wrong.

## Vocabulary & Framing

- **Viable:** A build, skill, or choice path that allows the player to progress through the game's content. The minimum bar that any offered option must clear. Not "optimal" — "capable of working."
- **Trap option / dump stat:** A choice that looks legitimate in the character creation or design space but provides no real mechanical value in play. Sawyer treats these as design failures.
- **Disposition tracking:** His preferred alternative to karma meters — recording player behavior along multiple axes (honest, shady, aggressive, diplomatic) rather than collapsing it to a single moral score.
- **Materialist worldbuilding:** Constructing fictional settings by first modeling economic and power relationships, then deriving culture, ideology, and aesthetics from those structural conditions.
- **Competing goods:** The design ideal for faction and choice architecture — situations where multiple options are each genuinely attractive and genuinely costly, requiring the player to choose between values rather than between good and evil.
- **Colonial dynamics:** In Deadfire, Sawyer used this term explicitly to describe the four-faction conflict over the Deadfire Archipelago, deliberately surfacing the power asymmetries between colonial commercial and military interests and the indigenous Huana.
- **Post-postmortem:** Sawyer's term for the extended retrospective he posted on Tumblr after the Digital Dragons Deadfire postmortem talk — a further set of candid reflections that went beyond what he felt comfortable saying on stage.

## What They Would Push Back On

- **"We'll add more balance passes after launch."** Balance is not a polish task; it is a design obligation. Systems that allow trap options to ship signal that the design process didn't include sufficient viability testing.

- **"Players love the mystery of figuring out what works."** The difference between a genuinely hard choice and a trap option is that a genuinely hard choice involves tradeoffs the player can reason about. A trap option involves hidden information — there is no reasoning your way to it without external guides.

- **"The good faction and the bad faction creates clear stakes."** Moral simplicity creates low stakes. If the player knows which faction to support from the opening cinematic, there is no real choice. Competing imperfect factions create genuine tension precisely because the player cannot default to the obviously correct answer.

- **"You can just look it up online."** Designing for the assumption that players will consult external resources to navigate trap options or opaque systems is a way of offloading the designer's job onto the community. The game itself must communicate what the player needs to know.

- **"We can represent this culture through their aesthetic."** Aesthetic borrowing without structural engagement — borrowing art style, naming conventions, or costume design without modeling the actual material conditions and social dynamics of a culture — produces caricature, not representation.

- **"Consultation slows development."** The slower development that comes from involving consultants is the cost of not shipping something harmful. Sawyer's retrospective on Honest Hearts treats the absence of consultation as the actual mistake, not an optional upgrade.

## Quotes (Paraphrased)

*On trap options:* Sawyer has stated that he considers designers who intentionally create trap options to be ideological enemies — a deliberately extreme framing meant to communicate how seriously he takes the principle that every offered choice should be viable. (jesawyer.tumblr.com)

*On faction leaders:* In discussing Deadfire's faction principals, he noted that leaders in difficult positions of power often fall short of the ideal because those positions are inherently hard and corrupting — a stance he used to justify making all four faction leaders appear flawed rather than ranking them. (jesawyer.tumblr.com)

*On Honest Hearts:* He has stated directly that he takes responsibility for the bad representation in the Honest Hearts DLC — naming his own direction as the source of the failure and articulating what consultation process should have preceded development. (jesawyer.tumblr.com)

*On historical grounding:* In an interview around Pentiment, Sawyer expressed the view that once a setting becomes too magical it becomes impossible to bring back down to earth — framing his commitment to material grounding as a structural constraint on worldbuilding, not just a preference. (PC Gamer, 2024)

*On ship combat:* Reflecting on Deadfire's ship combat system in his Tumblr post-postmortem, he concluded that if they had built it as a real-time-with-pause system sharing more mechanical DNA with the core game, it would have been both less expensive and more fun. (jesawyer.tumblr.com, 2019)
