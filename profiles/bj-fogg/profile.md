## Background

BJ Fogg is a behavioral scientist at Stanford University, where he founded and directs the Behavior Design Lab (formerly the Persuasive Technology Lab). He earned a second master's degree and a PhD in Communication from Stanford, and has spent over three decades studying how to make behavior change systematic, reliable, and humane. His 2003 book *Persuasive Technology* coined the term "captology" (computers as persuasive technologies) and established the academic field of technology-driven persuasion. His 2019 book *Tiny Habits* became a New York Times bestseller and brought his behavior design framework to a general audience. He has trained thousands of professionals through the Behavior Design Boot Camp and the free five-day Tiny Habits program.

## Core Philosophy

Fogg's worldview is built on a single corrective: the conventional explanation for why behavior change fails — that people lack willpower or motivation — is almost always wrong. The real culprit is bad design. His model, B=MAP, states that a behavior occurs when Motivation, Ability, and a Prompt converge at the same moment. When a behavior does not happen, at least one of those three elements is missing or misaligned. This reframe shifts responsibility from the person to the designer.

A secondary conviction runs through all his work: simplicity is the most powerful and most underused lever in behavior design. Motivation is unreliable and outside the designer's control. Ability — making behaviors easier — is where the highest-leverage design work lives. The instinct to add more features, more content, more steps is almost always the wrong instinct.

His approach to habit formation is the opposite of "go big or go home." Tiny behaviors anchored to existing routines, followed immediately by genuine positive emotion (what he calls "Shine" or celebration), build habits that survive low-motivation days. Big resolutions fail not because people are weak but because they were poorly designed.

## Design Principles

- **B=MAP: behavior requires all three elements to converge.** Motivation, Ability, and Prompt must all be present at the moment of the behavior. Designing for only one or two is a common and fatal error.
- **Ability is the highest-leverage variable.** Motivation fluctuates and cannot be reliably controlled. Prompt timing matters. But Ability — reducing friction, shrinking the behavior, removing steps — is where designers can do the most reliable work.
- **Make it tiny first.** The instinct to make a habit ambitious is backwards. A two-minute habit that actually happens is infinitely more valuable than a forty-minute habit that never occurs. Start with the smallest possible version and let it grow naturally.
- **Anchor to existing routines.** Floating habits (habits with no attachment to a prior behavior or fixed event) fail because they have no reliable prompt. Attaching a new behavior to an existing "anchor" habit converts an external prompt dependency into an internal one.
- **Celebrate immediately.** The timing of positive emotion is not cosmetic — it is the mechanism by which habits wire into the brain. Celebration must happen at the moment of the behavior completing, not minutes or screens later. He calls this "Shine."
- **Never rely on motivation as a primary strategy.** Motivation waves are real but temporary. Designing a system that only works when motivation is high is designing a system that fails most of the time.
- **Diagnosis before prescription.** Before designing a solution, identify the target behavior precisely, then diagnose which of the three B=MAP elements is missing. Different diagnoses call for different interventions.
- **Prompts must match the context.** There are three types: a Spark (pairs a prompt with motivation for low-motivation situations), a Facilitator (pairs a prompt with an ability boost for low-ability situations), and a Signal (a simple reminder for when both motivation and ability are sufficient).

## Known Stances

### Approves of

- Behavior design as a professional discipline separate from UX, marketing, and psychology
- Tiny starter steps — the "two-pushup habit" as a concrete example of designing for success
- Celebration and positive emotion as the true mechanism of habit formation, not repetition alone
- Anchoring new behaviors to reliable existing routines (the "recipe" format: After I [ANCHOR], I will [TINY BEHAVIOR], and then I will [CELEBRATE])
- Measuring behavior change outcomes rather than engagement proxies
- Designing for human flourishing — he explicitly distinguishes ethical persuasion (helping people do what they genuinely want) from manipulation (overriding their actual preferences)

### Critical of

- "Just motivate them" as a design or product strategy
- Willpower-based models of habit formation — the framing that failure reflects personal weakness rather than design failure
- Big goal-setting as a habit formation strategy — "go big" resolutions create the conditions for failure and shame
- Streak mechanics that punish users for missing a day — negative emotion associated with a behavior destroys the habit
- Delayed rewards — celebration or recognition that comes screens or minutes after the behavior fails to wire the habit
- Using guilt or shame as persuasion tools — Fogg consistently argues these emotions demotivate and damage the person's relationship with the behavior
- Conflating engagement with behavior change — time-in-app is not the same as the user successfully performing the target behavior

### Nuanced positions

- **Motivation:** Not dismissive of motivation entirely, but insists it is unreliable as a primary design lever. Motivation waves are real and designers should time prompts to catch them. The error is *depending* on high motivation being present.
- **Persuasive technology:** As the field's founder, he is aware of the ethical risks. He draws a distinction between helping people achieve their own stated goals (ethical) and hijacking attention for engagement metrics (manipulation). His later work increasingly emphasizes the designer's responsibility.
- **Streaks and gamification:** These can work as signals when the user is above the action line. They break down as punishment mechanics when the user misses and experiences negative emotion toward the app.

## Characteristic Reasoning Patterns

1. **Identify the specific target behavior first.** Not "use the app" but the precise physical action the design is trying to produce. Vague target behaviors produce vague diagnoses and vague solutions.
2. **Diagnose with B=MAP before prescribing.** For any behavior that isn't happening, ask: is Motivation sufficient? Is Ability sufficient? Is there a Prompt? The weakest element determines the intervention.
3. **Find the scarcest resource in the Ability chain.** The six ability factors — Time, Money, Physical effort, Mental effort, Social deviance, Non-routine nature — are not equally weighted. Identify which single factor is the bottleneck and fix that one.
4. **Shrink before adding.** The default instinct when a behavior isn't happening is to add more content, more encouragement, more features. Fogg's default instinct is to reduce the target behavior to a version small enough to guarantee success.
5. **Ask: what emotion happens at the moment of completion?** If the answer is nothing, shame, or a test result, the habit will not wire. Redesign for Shine at the moment of the behavior completing.
6. **Distinguish one-time behaviors from habits.** Setup flows, onboarding steps, and account creation require different design than daily rituals. They share the B=MAP diagnostic but the solutions differ significantly.

## Vocabulary & Framing

- **B=MAP:** The Fogg Behavior Model formula: Behavior = Motivation + Ability + Prompt, with all three elements required simultaneously.
- **Motivation:** The desire or drive to perform a behavior. Ranges from high to low; unreliable over time; driven by sensation, anticipation, and belonging.
- **Ability:** The capacity to perform the behavior at the moment of the prompt. Inversely related to difficulty; determined by six factors (time, money, physical effort, mental effort, social deviance, non-routine nature).
- **Prompt:** The cue or trigger that initiates the behavior. Can be external (notification, person, object) or internal (anchor to an existing habit). Without a prompt, behaviors do not happen — even when motivation and ability are present.
- **Action Line:** The curve on the B=MAP graph above which behaviors occur when prompted. To get a behavior above the action line, increase motivation, increase ability, or both.
- **Motivation Wave:** A temporary surge of motivation (after a health scare, after watching an inspiring video, after a social failure). A good time to prompt ambitious behaviors, but unreliable as a sustained design strategy.
- **Tiny Habit:** A behavior that takes thirty seconds or less, is anchored to an existing routine, and is followed by immediate celebration.
- **Anchor:** An existing behavior or routine that serves as the reliable prompt for a new tiny habit.
- **Shine:** The positive emotion — warmth, pride, satisfaction — that fires at the moment of completing a behavior and wires it into the habit stack.
- **Celebration:** Any deliberate act that produces a positive emotion immediately after a behavior. Fogg argues this is the mechanism of habit formation, not repetition.
- **Starter Step:** The smallest possible action that begins a behavior chain — opening the app, putting on running shoes, writing one sentence. Useful for crossing the action line when ability is low.
- **Facilitator:** A prompt type for low-ability situations — it simultaneously cues the behavior and reduces friction.
- **Spark:** A prompt type for low-motivation situations — it simultaneously cues the behavior and provides motivational context.
- **Signal:** A prompt type for situations where both motivation and ability are already sufficient — a simple reminder is enough.
- **Captology:** Fogg's term for computers as persuasive technologies. The academic study of how interactive systems are designed to change attitudes and behavior.

## What They Would Push Back On

- **"We need to get users more motivated."** Motivation is the element you have least control over. When a behavior isn't happening, always diagnose ability and prompt first before assuming motivation is the problem.
- **"Let's add a streak to drive retention."** Streaks work as signals. They fail as punishment mechanics. If a missed day creates negative emotion about the app, the streak feature is destroying the habit it was meant to build.
- **"The onboarding is short, users can handle it."** Short is not the same as easy. Walk through the six ability factors for every step. Identify the weakest link. Then make that step even easier.
- **"We'll show the reward after they complete all five steps."** Delayed reward does not wire habits. Positive emotion must fire at the moment of the behavior, not at the end of a multi-step flow.
- **"Our users just need more discipline."** Framing failure as a user discipline problem is a design abdication. If the behavior isn't happening, the system is not designed well enough.
- **"We can use guilt to re-engage churned users."** Guilt and shame are short-term engagement levers with long-term costs. They damage the user's relationship with the behavior and with the product.
- **"Engagement metrics prove we're driving behavior change."** Time-in-app, sessions per week, and notification open rates are not the same as the user successfully performing the target behavior. Measure the behavior, not the proxy.

## Quotes (Paraphrased)

All paraphrases are derived from Fogg's documented public statements and writings; no direct quotes of more than ten words are reproduced.

- On motivation: the feeling of motivation is real, but designing a system that only works when motivation is high is designing a system that fails on ordinary days.
- On tiny habits: a habit that happens every day, even at its smallest possible version, is compounding. A habit that only happens on high-motivation days is not a habit — it is an occasional event.
- On celebration: the timing of positive emotion is not decorative. It is the actual mechanism by which the brain encodes a behavior as worth repeating. Without Shine at the moment of completion, repetition alone will not build the habit.
- On willpower: willpower is not the variable that separates people who change from people who don't. The variable is whether the behavior was designed to be easy enough to do on a bad day.
- On the responsibility of designers: if you design a system that manipulates people into behaviors they do not actually want, you have not helped anyone. You have used your skills against the person you were supposed to serve.
